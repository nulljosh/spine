## Part 1: An Overview of Calculus

**Ch.1 — What Is Calculus?** Calculus is basically algebra and geometry with the added element of infinity — it studies curved, changing things by imagining them broken into infinitely many infinitesimally small straight pieces. Real-world examples given: calculating the cable length needed for a suspension bridge and the amount of power needed to launch a rocket to Mars — both involve continuously changing quantities that regular algebra/geometry can't handle directly.

**Ch.2 — The Two Big Ideas of Calculus.** Differentiation finds a curve's slope (rate of change) at a point — the derivative is both "a slope and a rate." Integration is the reverse process: adding up infinitely many infinitesimal pieces to find totals like area under a curve. Infinite series are introduced via Zeno's Achilles-and-the-tortoise paradox: an infinite sum of shrinking time intervals (10 + 1 + 0.1 + 0.01 + ...) can still add up to a finite total (11.11... seconds) — resolving the paradox and previewing convergent vs. divergent series.

**Ch.3 — Why Calculus Works.** The "limit" is described as calculus's microscope — zooming in on a curve until it looks locally straight (same idea as the Earth looking flat at human scale despite being round). This zooming-in process, formalized as a limit, is what lets you apply ordinary straight-line algebra/geometry (slope, area, Pythagorean theorem) to curves.

## Part 2: Warming Up with Calculus Prerequisites

**Ch.4 — Pre-Algebra and Algebra Review.** Refresher on fraction arithmetic (multiplying, dividing, adding/subtracting, canceling), absolute value, exponent rules (negative and fractional powers), root rules, and logarithms (defined via the exponential relationship, e.g. log₁₀1000 = 3 because 10³ = 1000). Covers factoring techniques (GCF, difference of squares, difference/sum of cubes, trinomial factoring by grouping/trial) and three methods for solving quadratic equations: factoring, the quadratic formula, and completing the square.

**Ch.5 — Funky Functions and Their Groovy Graphs.** A function has exactly one output for each input — the chapter's core defining rule. Covers function notation f(x), independent vs. dependent variables, composite functions (f(g(x))), and reading graphs to identify functions via the vertical line test. Covers lines (slope-intercept and point-slope forms, parallel lines have equal slopes, perpendicular lines have negative reciprocal slopes), parabolas and other even/odd functions (symmetric about the y-axis vs. the origin), and exponential, logarithmic, and inverse functions (inverse functions are reflections of each other across the line y = x).

**Ch.6 — The Trig Tango.** Right-triangle trig via SOHCAHTOA (sine, cosine, tangent and their reciprocals cosecant, secant, cotangent). Memorize the 30-60-90 and 45-45-90 special right triangles. Introduces the unit circle and radian measure (2π radians = 360°), reading sin/cos/tan values around all four quadrants using reference angles, key trig identities (sin²x + cos²x = 1, sin2x = 2 sinx cosx), graphing sine/cosine/tangent (period, amplitude), and inverse trig functions (restricted ranges needed so they qualify as true functions).

## Part 3: Limits

**Ch.7 — Limits and Continuity.** Formal limit definition: a limit exists at x = c if the function gets closer and closer to a single value as x approaches c from both the left and right, regardless of the function's actual value (or non-existence) at c. Covers one-sided limits, limits at removable holes (the limit exists even where the function is undefined), vertical asymptotes (limits at infinity from a jump/break), the formal epsilon-delta definition, and the "33333 limit mnemonic" tying together the 3 requirements each for a limit to exist, for continuity, and for a derivative to exist. Continuity requires: the function is defined at c, the limit exists at c, and the limit equals the function's value at c.

**Ch.8 — Evaluating Limits.** Techniques in order of preference: (1) plug-and-chug direct substitution for continuous functions; (2) calculator/table methods for a numeric estimate; (3) algebraic techniques when substitution gives 0/0 — factoring and canceling, or multiplying by the conjugate for expressions with square roots; (4) the "squeeze/sandwich theorem" for oscillating functions bounded between two other functions with the same limit; (5) limits at infinity for rational functions, determined by comparing the degrees of numerator and denominator (higher denominator degree → limit is 0; equal degrees → limit is the ratio of leading coefficients; higher numerator degree → limit is ±∞).

## Part 4: Differentiation

**Ch.9 — Differentiation Orientation.** The derivative is defined as the limit of the difference quotient: f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) − f(x)]/h — the slope of the secant line between two points as they merge into one tangent point. Builds this from average rate of change (slope of a secant line) to instantaneous rate of change (slope of the tangent line, i.e., the derivative). Three cases where a derivative fails to exist: at any discontinuity, at a sharp corner/cusp, or at a vertical tangent line (undefined slope).

**Ch.10 — Differentiation Rules.** The core toolkit: constant rule (derivative of a constant is 0), power rule (d/dx xⁿ = nxⁿ⁻¹), constant multiple rule, sum/difference rule, product rule ((fg)' = f'g + fg'), quotient rule ((f/g)' = (f'g − fg')/g²), and the chain rule for composite functions (differentiate the outside function leaving the inside alone, then multiply by the derivative of the inside). Also covers implicit differentiation (for equations not solved for y), logarithmic differentiation (useful for products/quotients/powers of functions), derivatives of exponential and logarithmic functions, derivatives of trig and inverse trig functions, and higher-order derivatives (repeatedly differentiating: velocity → acceleration → jerk).

**Ch.11 — Differentiation and the Shape of Curves.** Uses the "hill and valley" road-trip metaphor: local maxima/minima occur at critical points (where the derivative is 0 or undefined). The first derivative test (sign changes from + to − at a max, − to + at a min) and second derivative test (positive second derivative = concave up = local min; negative = concave down = local max) both locate extrema. Covers concavity, inflection points (where concavity changes, second derivative = 0 or undefined), finding absolute extrema over a closed interval (check critical points plus both endpoints), and reading the shape of a function's graph directly from its derivative's graph.

**Ch.12 — Your Problems Are Solved: Differentiation to the Rescue!** Real-world applications: optimization problems (finding maximum/minimum values — e.g. the box or corral with the largest volume/area for fixed material), position/velocity/acceleration problems (velocity is the derivative of position, acceleration is the derivative of velocity; total distance traveled vs. displacement differ when velocity changes sign), and related rates problems (using implicit differentiation and the chain rule to relate how multiple changing quantities affect each other over time — classic examples: inflating balloon, filling trough, two cars moving apart).

**Ch.13 — More Differentiation Problems: Going Off on a Tangent.** Tangent line and normal line problems (the normal line is perpendicular to the tangent at a point), linear approximation (using the tangent line as a simple local estimate of a curve near a point), and business/economics applications (marginal cost/revenue/profit are the derivatives of the cost/revenue/profit functions — the rate of change per additional unit produced; maximum profit occurs where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, i.e., where profit's derivative is zero).

## Part 5: Integration and Infinite Series

**Ch.14 — Intro to Integration and Approximating Area.** Integration is "fancy addition" — finding the area under a curve by summing infinitely many infinitesimally thin rectangles. Builds up estimation methods: left-rectangle sums, right-rectangle sums, and midpoint sums (increasingly accurate as the number of rectangles increases), formalized via sigma/summation notation and Riemann sums. The definite integral ∫[a,b] f(x)dx is defined as the limit of a Riemann sum as the number of rectangles approaches infinity. Also covers the trapezoid rule and Simpson's rule as more accurate area-approximation shortcuts that don't require the full limit process.

**Ch.15 — Integration: It's Backwards Differentiation.** The key insight: integration (finding areas) is the reverse of differentiation, formalized in the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, which has two parts — Part 1 connects the area function's derivative back to the original function; Part 2 (the practical one) says the definite integral of f from a to b equals F(b) − F(a), where F is any antiderivative of f. This turns area-finding from a tedious limit calculation into simple antiderivative evaluation. Covers antidifferentiation rules (reverse power rule, reverse rules for trig/exponential functions), the "+C" constant of integration (since antiderivatives aren't unique), guess-and-check antidifferentiation, and the substitution method (u-substitution) for antidifferentiating composite functions — the integration analog of the chain rule.

## TL;DR (of what's covered so far)

The book builds from why calculus exists (Part 1) through an algebra/trig refresher (Part 2) into limits — calculus's foundational "microscope" concept for handling infinity rigorously (Part 3). Part 4 covers differentiation: building the derivative from slopes of secant lines to tangent lines, the full toolkit of differentiation rules (power, product, quotient, chain, implicit, logarithmic), using derivatives to map a curve's shape (extrema, concavity, inflection points), and real-world applications (optimization, motion, related rates, business marginals). Part 5 flips to integration: approximating area under curves with rectangle sums, then the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus revealing that integration is just differentiation in reverse — collapsing what would be a tedious limit calculation into simple antiderivative evaluation, plus the substitution method for handling composite functions.
