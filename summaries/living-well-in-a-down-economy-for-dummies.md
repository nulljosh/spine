## Introduction

A recession is officially "a decline in GDP lasting two or more consecutive quarters," but the book's real focus is what you do about it regardless of the official label. Four objectives frame the whole book:
- Prepare yourself in case you lose your job.
- Take control of your finances — live within your means and build savings.
- Continue to enjoy your life while maintaining financial control (don't just deprive yourself — spend smarter).
- Have a plan for worst-case scenarios like bankruptcy or foreclosure.

The book is organized as standalone numbered tips you can read in any order — jump to what's relevant via the Table of Contents or index rather than reading cover to cover.

## Part 1: Facing Off Against a Recession

### #1 — Determining Your Net Worth
Net worth = assets − liabilities. Use the worksheet categories: cash accounts (checking, savings, money market, cash on hand), personal use assets (residence, vehicles, personal property), investment assets (retirement accounts, brokerage, mutual funds, other), against liabilities (mortgage, home equity loans/lines, auto loans, investment/margin loans, credit card debt, other loans). Update this figure periodically — it's your financial starting point and the way to track progress.

### #2 — Calculating Spendable Income
Spendable income ≠ gross salary. Start from gross income per pay period, subtract taxes (federal, state, local/city, FICA) and automatic payroll deductions (401(k)/retirement, health/dental/disability/life insurance, HSA/FSA contributions, union dues, stock purchase plans), then multiply by pay periods per year to get annual spendable income.

### #3 — Calculating Required Expenses
Required expenses = the bills that don't stop regardless of income: protection (health, life, disability, renter's/homeowner's insurance), housing (rent/mortgage, utilities, phone/internet), basic food and hygiene, and minimum debt payments. Distinguish these from discretionary spending — this is the "floor" you must cover before anything else.

### #4 — Calculating Discretionary Spending
Discretionary categories include entertainment, clothing/personal care beyond basics, gifts, dining out, subscriptions, and "other" (auto/parking beyond commuting need, misc.). Total discretionary spending against your after-tax income minus required expenses reveals your surplus — or shortfall. If there's a shortfall, this table shows exactly where to trim first.

### #5 — Tracking Expenses
Track spending for at least a couple of months before drawing conclusions — a short window rarely reflects true patterns. Any tracking method works (notebook, spreadsheet, app) as long as you're consistent; the value is in seeing where money actually goes versus where you think it goes.

### #6 — Setting Spending Practices
Long-term financial planning starts with defining your dreams (retirement, education, big purchases) and converting them into concrete goals using the "SMART" approach — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound. Steps: (1) brainstorm dreams, (2) determine which dreams matter most, (3) develop an action plan with a timeline and priority order.

### #7 — Creating a Budget
Three-step process:
- **Step 1 — Categorize your expenses**: group into medical/insurance, housing, food, transportation, personal, entertainment, gifts/donations, education, and savings/retirement.
- **Step 2 — Estimate what you spend**: for each category, tally recent statements/receipts.
- **Step 3 — Calculate and adjust**: compare income to spending; if expenses exceed income, cut discretionary categories first and set a target savings percentage.

### #8 — Breathing Away Tension
Simple breathing techniques for acute financial stress: breathe through your nose (slower, calmer than mouth-breathing), diaphragmatic breathing (hand on chest, hand on belly — only the belly hand should move), and "the quickie" — 3 slow breaths held a few seconds each, usable anywhere including mid-crisis.

### #9 — Tensing Your Way to Relaxation
Progressive muscle relaxation: tense a muscle group hard for ~10 seconds, then release and notice the contrast. Work through body parts sequentially (feet, legs, abdomen, chest, arms, hands, face) or use the "quickie" version — tense everything at once for 5 seconds, release, repeat.

### #10 — Stretching Away Your Stress
Target common stress-holding areas — neck, shoulders, back, jaw — with short stretches held 15-30 seconds. Gentle range-of-motion neck rolls, shoulder shrugs/rolls, and stretching hands/wrists after screen-heavy stretches of work.

### #11 — Practicing Habits of Effective Stress Managers
Recurring traits: exercise regularly, sleep enough, engage in a hobby, maintain a strong support system (friends/family/community — don't isolate under stress), stay organized (clutter and disorganization compound stress), manage time deliberately (calendars, lists), live according to your own values rather than others' expectations, and keep a sense of humor.

## Part 2: Bumping Up Your Bring-Home

### #12 — Updating Your Resume
Build a **core resume** first — a base document with contact info, summary, key skills, education, experience, activities/affiliations, honors — then customize ("spin off") targeted versions per job application rather than rewriting from scratch each time. Gather source material first: past resumes, employment records, training certificates, recommendation letters, project docs, and publications.

### #13 — Choosing the Right Resume Format
Three formats:
| Situation | Suggested format |
|---|---|
| Perfect career progression | Reverse chronological |
| New graduate | Chrono-functional |
| Seasoned professional | Reverse chronological, hybrid if gaps |
| Military transition | Reverse chronological or chrono-functional |
| Job history gaps | Chrono-functional or hybrid |
| Career change | Hybrid, sometimes reverse chronological |
| Demotions | Any |

Avoid a purely **functional** format (skills-only, no company names/dates) — it's a red flag to employers and should never be used.

### #14 — Making Your Resume Stand Out
- **Sell it, don't tell it**: replace "Responsible for X" with results — quantify impact, answer the implicit "so what?"
- **Use keywords**: employers and applicant tracking systems search for job-specific keywords (skills, certifications, tools, big-name employers) — tailor keywords per job ad rather than reusing a generic list.
- **Drop articles** (a, an, the) and "I" — resumes read as compact phrases, not full sentences.
- **Delete the leave-outs**: no "Objective" header, no "References available on request," no SSN/DOB, no old dates (10-15+ years), no irrelevant activities (political/religious affiliations, anything that invites bias).

### #15 — Preparing for a Job Interview
Don't over-script answers — sound natural, not memorized. Prepare: appropriate attire (one notch above the job's dress code), answers to "Tell me about yourself" (start from your first career-relevant moment, not birth), 2-3 talking points that differentiate you, and three ready PAR (Problem-Approach-Result) stories, 30-60 seconds each, drawn from work, school, or extracurriculars.

### #16 — Standing Out During an Interview
Good posture, firm handshake, genuine smile. Listen more than you talk — the "traffic light rule": first 30 seconds they're fully engaged (green), next 30 seconds attention starts to wane (yellow), past 1 minute you've likely said enough (red) — stop or ask a question. For panel interviews, address the asker first, then briefly meet others' eyes before returning. Prepare a few smart questions for when they ask if you have any — e.g., "What would you want me to accomplish in the first month?"

### #17 — Dealing with Video Interviews
Send materials in advance, test tech beforehand, frame the camera head-and-shoulders with no shadows, keep movements calm, look near the camera lens (not the screen) for eye contact, and treat the sign-off like a real handshake — say something warm and let the interviewer end the call first. Some interviews are now asynchronous (prerecorded questions, recorded answers) — same prep applies.

### #18 — Employing Post-Interview Tactics
Skip the generic thank-you note in favor of an **influencing letter**: reiterate enthusiasm, add new information you didn't get to mention, take a second pass at a question you fumbled, and optionally attach relevant collateral (writing samples, articles, references). Follow up a few days after the promised decision date if you haven't heard back — reaching out shows genuine interest without seeming desperate, as long as you don't do it too soon.

### #19 — Trying Quick Ways to Find Good Jobs
**Call-email-call**: leave a voicemail, immediately follow with an email, then call again a few days later — works even for cold outreach to employers not actively advertising. Aim broadly (10-20 target employers); rejection is expected and fine. **Phone a friend**: your network is more persuasive by voice than by text/email — call (don't just message) a dozen or so contacts who could plausibly know of an opening.

### #20 — Looking [Elsewhere for Job Sources] / Blast Your Social Media
Push your job search out through LinkedIn and other social networks — connect with organizations and individuals in your target field, engage authentically (comments, thoughtful posts) rather than cold-pitching immediately, then ask for referrals once you've built some visibility.

## TL;DR

When money's tight: figure out your real net worth and true spendable income first, separate required expenses from discretionary ones so you know exactly where the fat is, track spending for real (not from memory), and turn vague financial worries into a concrete budget and action plan. Manage the stress side deliberately — breathing, progressive relaxation, staying organized, and leaning on your support system all measurably help. If income needs to go up, treat your resume as a living "core" document you customize per job, use keywords and results (not vague duty statements) to stand out, over-prepare for interviews without sounding scripted, follow up with an influencing letter instead of a generic thank-you, and use call-email-call plus your network (phone, not just text) to surface opportunities faster than job boards alone.
