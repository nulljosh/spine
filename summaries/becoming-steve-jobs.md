# Becoming Steve Jobs

*By Brent Schlender and Rick Tetzeli. Note: this scan covers the Authors' Note, Prologue, Chapter 1 ("Steve Jobs in the Garden of Allah"), Chapter 2 ("I Didn't Want to Be a Businessman"), Chapter 3 ("Breakthrough and Breakdown"), and the first half of Chapter 4 ("What's Next?") — roughly the first 129 pages of a 437-page, 17-chapter book. Chapters 4 (remainder) through 17 — the NeXT years continuing, the Pixar/Toy Story era, the Apple return, iMac/iPod/iPhone/iPad launches, and Jobs's final years and legacy — were not photographed in this batch.*

## Authors' Note & Prologue

Journalists Brent Schlender and Rick Tetzeli covered Jobs for decades (Schlender for *Fortune*) and had an unusually personal, direct relationship with him — the book draws on that access rather than secondhand reporting. The Prologue recalls Schlender's first encounter with Jobs in 1986: notoriously combative in interviews, but the book's throughline is how Jobs changed dramatically between that first meeting and his death in 2011 — from a mercurial, difficult young founder into a more patient, empathetic leader. The authors argue Jobs's most productive, most human decade was actually his last, following his return to Apple.

## Chapter 1 — Steve Jobs in the Garden of Allah

Opens in December 1979 with Jobs arriving late and furious to an Apple board retreat chaired by investor Arthur Rock, who found Jobs's temperament grating and doubted him early on. The chapter backfills Jobs's origin story: adopted, raised in Mountain View at the literal edge of Silicon Valley's semiconductor boom, obsessed early with Hewlett-Packard's culture and the Homebrew Computer Club. Meets Steve Wozniak in high school; the two bond over electronics, phone phreaking (the "blue box"), and shared irreverence. Details Jobs's early jobs (including at Atari under Al Alcorn, and a formative pilgrimage to India in 1974 that shaped his aesthetic and spiritual sensibility — Zen Buddhism, minimalism). Apple I and Apple II are built essentially in the Jobs family garage with Wozniak doing the engineering and Jobs pushing distribution, financing, and design sensibility. Early investor Mike Markkula becomes a crucial partner, providing capital and adult supervision; marketing guru Regis McKenna helps professionalize Apple's image.

## Chapter 2 — "I Didn't Want to Be a Businessman"

Apple's runaway success with Apple II (helped by VisiCalc, the first killer spreadsheet app) funds ambitious next projects. Jobs, still in his early twenties and uninterested in conventional management, gravitates toward product vision instead. Key beat: the 1979 visit to Xerox PARC, where Jobs and an Apple team see the GUI, mouse, and networked personal computer for the first time — directly inspiring the Lisa and later the Macintosh. Internally, Apple is scaling fast and chaotically; professional executives (including CEO Mike Scott, later Markkula himself) are brought in to impose structure Jobs resists. The chapter also covers Apple's 1980 IPO, instantly creating enormous paper wealth, and the widening gap between the freewheeling early culture and the corporation Apple was becoming.

## Chapter 3 — Breakthrough and Breakdown

Covers parallel personal and professional crises. Personally: Jobs denies paternity of his daughter Lisa Brennan (born 1978) for years despite a DNA test, a widely cited low point in his personal life addressed candidly in the book. Professionally: the Apple III launches with serious hardware problems and flops, denting Apple's momentum and credibility just as competition intensifies (IBM's PC launch in 1981 is a major threat). Jobs is pushed off the Lisa project internally and pivots his energy to the small, scrappy Macintosh team, envisioning it as an affordable, elegant "computer for the rest of us." He recruits an eclectic team, drives them hard, and effectively willed the Mac into existence on a tight budget and timeline, borrowing liberally from PARC's GUI concepts. The chapter closes as Sculley — recruited from Pepsi in 1983 specifically at Jobs's urging with the famous "sugar water" pitch — is brought in as CEO to provide the business discipline Jobs still lacked, setting up the power struggle to come.

## Chapter 4 — What's Next? (partial — pages 92–129)

Opens with the fallout from the 1985 boardroom coup: after a power struggle with Sculley over Apple's direction (the Mac wasn't selling as hoped and internal politics turned against Jobs), the board sides with Sculley and Jobs is stripped of operational authority, then resigns from Apple entirely. He describes it later as devastating but ultimately liberating — "the sense of freedom... allowed me to enter one of the most creative periods of my life." Jobs immediately founds **NeXT**, aiming to build high-end computers for higher education and research, targeting elegance and technical ambition (custom silicon, object-oriented software, the NeXTcube's magnesium case) over near-term commercial pragmatism. Recruits several loyal ex-Apple engineers. Ross Perot becomes a major investor. The chapter details NeXT's expensive, perfectionist culture (a lavish Redwood City HQ, a famous Paul Rand-designed logo commissioned for $100,000, an extremely costly automated factory), and its persistent commercial struggles — the NeXT computer ships late (1988) at a high price with limited software, and sales badly undershoot expectations. Meanwhile Jobs personally invests in a small computer-graphics division spun out of Lucasfilm — the company that would become Pixar — mostly viewing it initially as a hardware play rather than an entertainment company. IBM briefly considers and then rejects licensing NeXTSTEP as it goes all-in on its own OS/2, and Bill Gates/Microsoft's rise (DOS/Windows, and later graphical Windows) increasingly dominates the industry NeXT was trying to disrupt from the high end.

## TL;DR (of what's covered so far)

Steve Jobs's twenties: co-founds Apple from a garage with Wozniak and turns Homebrew-club hobbyist tinkering into the first mass-market personal computer company, aided decisively by Mike Markkula's money/mentorship and Regis McKenna's marketing polish. The 1979 Xerox PARC visit reshapes his idea of what a computer could be, feeding into both the Lisa and the Macintosh he later champions after being pushed off Lisa. Apple's early triumphs (Apple II, IPO) are shadowed by real failures (Apple III) and personal ones (years of denying his daughter Lisa). Bringing in Sculley as CEO to grow up the company backfires on Jobs personally — a 1985 boardroom battle costs him control of Apple and he's forced out. He responds by throwing himself into NeXT (elegant, expensive, and commercially underwhelming) and a side bet on a small graphics outfit that would eventually become Pixar — though at this point in the book it's still a minor sideline, not yet the second act it will become.
