## Chapter 1: Introducing Exercise Science
Exercise science studies the body's acute responses and chronic adaptations to physical activity — how the body initiates and controls movement via the nervous, cardiovascular, and metabolic systems.

**Foundational sciences it draws on:** biology, anatomy, physiology, chemistry, biochemistry, physics, and psychology.

**Key subdisciplines:**
- **Biomechanics** — the mechanics of exercise/movement (forces, levers, balance, acceleration), measured via force platforms and electromyography.
- **Exercise physiology** — the body in motion; how organ systems (muscular, cardiovascular) function and adapt to training; common in clinical/sports-medicine settings.
- **Fitness and wellness** — using exercise/nutrition to reduce disease risk ("Exercise is Medicine" framing).
- **Graduate-level health professions** — athletic training, physical therapy, occupational therapy, physician assistant studies, public health.
- **Rehabilitation therapy** — exercise for recovery from injury/illness.
- **Sports and exercise psychology** — mood, behavior, confidence, and mental performance; recommends *Sports Psychology For Dummies* (Leif Smith & Todd Kays).
- **Strength and conditioning** — training principles to improve performance and resist fatigue.

**Body systems covered by the field:** brain/nervous system, circulatory system, cardiorespiratory system, skeletal system, muscular system, energy/metabolism/nutrition, and the endocrine system (hormonal control, e.g. insulin enabling glucose uptake).

**Is this field for you?** The book suggests asking: Do I enjoy exercise? Do I like helping people? Do I want to know the science behind exercise? Do I want to use exercise to improve the human condition?

## Chapter 2: Better Than a Pill — Exercise Is Medicine
- **Exercise is Medicine (EIM)**, launched 2007, is a joint initiative of the American College of Sports Medicine and the American Medical Association, active in 40+ countries, aiming to get physical activity assessed/prescribed like medication (using FITT: Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type).
- **Physical activity vs. exercise**: physical activity is any large-muscle movement that burns calories (e.g. mowing the lawn); exercise is *planned* activity with a fitness goal (e.g. going for a run).
- **METs (metabolic equivalents)** measure intensity: 1 MET = resting cost; light = 1.6-2.9 METs, moderate = 3.0-5.9, vigorous = 6.0+. Recommended volume: 450-900 MET-minutes/week. US guideline: 150 min/week moderate (or 75 min vigorous) aerobic activity — yet 80% of US adults don't meet it.
- **Sitting is the new smoking**: sitting 4-6 hrs/day raises death risk ~30%; 8+ hrs/day raises it ~60% vs. staying active.
- Hippocrates (460-370 BCE) is credited with an early "nourishment and exercise" health quote.
- **Disease-specific benefits**:
  - *Diabetes*: Type 2 involves insulin resistance; exercise triggers GLUT4 glucose transport into cells independent of insulin, functioning "almost like an insulin shot," reducing needed insulin doses. Type 1 diabetics also benefit for glucose control.
  - *Cancer*: physical activity linked to 10-20% lower rates for bladder, breast, colon, endometrial, esophageal, kidney, stomach cancers; up to 40% lower death-from-any-cause in already-diagnosed, physically active cancer patients; may ease chemotoxicity.
  - *Heart disease* (leading US killer, ~700,000 deaths/yr, ~1-in-5 deaths): exercise lowers blood pressure (~10mmHg with reduced body fat), lowers LDL cholesterol, strengthens heart muscle/stroke volume, and grows collateral coronary arteries.
  - *Metabolic syndrome* (co-occurring obesity, high BP, diabetes): treated by reducing body fat + increasing activity.
- **Health span vs. lifespan**: health span = years lived in good health; current US average lifespan ~79 yrs but health span only ~63 yrs. Exercise/diet extend health span, not just lifespan (Figure 2-2 shows active/good-diet individuals maintaining high health scores to ~80 vs. inactive/obese/diabetic individuals declining from ~age 40).
- Exercise also strengthens the immune system (notably relevant during COVID-19, where inactive/obese/diabetic/hypertensive people were highest-risk), preserves bone density (countering osteoporosis, esp. post-menopause), and "mends the brain" — improving cognitive function, motor coordination, memory, and brain volume via longer dendrites and increased BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), enhancing neuroplasticity even in older adults.

## The science of muscle contraction
- A muscle contraction starts in the brain's motor control centers, sending signals down the spinal cord to motor units.
- **Motor unit**: one nerve connected to a set number of muscle fibers (anywhere from ~100 to 1,000+ fibers per nerve) — this connection gives the brain graded control over contraction force.
- **Muscle structure hierarchy**: muscle → myofibrils → sarcomeres (the smallest contractile unit). Sarcomere parts: M line (central connective structure), I band (thin actin filaments only), A band (thick myosin filaments), zone of overlap (where actin/myosin interact), Z lines (anchor points connecting sarcomeres), actin (thin filaments), myosin filaments (thick, "rowboat oar" filaments that pull), titin (elastic protein holding myosin in place).
- **Calcium release**: stored in the sarcoplasmic reticulum, released via T-tubules when triggered by a motor-unit stimulus.
- **Binding site exposure**: tropomyosin normally covers actin's binding sites; troponin (activated by calcium) shifts tropomyosin out of the way to expose them.
- **Contraction sequence**: brain sends electrical stimulus → motor units relay signal to fibers → calcium releases from sarcoplasmic reticulum → calcium binds troponin, changing its shape → tropomyosin moves, exposing binding sites → myosin heads attach to actin → myosin heads rotate, pulling actin toward the sarcomere center → repeats as long as energy/stimulus continues.

## Fast-twitch vs. slow-twitch fibers
- **Slow-twitch** ("tortoise"): small nerve, low-threshold firing, connects to few fibers (good for fine motor control), aerobic metabolism, high mitochondria/myoglobin content (the "dark meat" pigment), fatigue-resistant but weaker. A marathoner's vastus lateralis may be ~95% slow-twitch vs. ~50% average.
- **Fast-twitch A** ("hare"): large nerve, high threshold, connects to thousands of fibers per nerve, anaerobic metabolism, produces more force and lactic acid, fatigues quickly.
- **Fast-twitch X (intermediate)**: hybrid — can use both aerobic and anaerobic metabolism; trainable toward either endurance (loses some sprint speed) or sprint (loses some endurance) characteristics depending on training type.
- Recruitment order: slow-twitch fibers recruited first (lowest threshold), then fast-twitch A, then fast-twitch X as force demand increases (Figure 3-6).

## Muscle architecture & behavior
- **Behavioral properties**: extensibility (stretch beyond resting length), elasticity (return to resting length after stretch), irritability/excitability (responsiveness to stimuli), contractility (ability to generate tension).
- **Structural categories**: parallel architecture (fibers run along muscle length — fusiform, triangular, flat, strap shapes; larger range of motion) vs. pennate architecture (fibers angle into tendons — unipennate/bipennate/multipennate; generates more force due to more fibers per tendon attachment).
- **Origin/insertion/action**: origin = less movable, more proximal attachment; insertion = more movable, more distal attachment; action = the movement produced.
- **Two-joint / multi-joint muscles**: e.g. biceps brachii (two-joint, spans shoulder+elbow) vs. erector spinae (multi-joint, spans the whole spine).
- **Gradation of force**: driven by neural signal size and motor-unit firing threshold; smaller/slow-twitch units recruited first, then larger/fast-twitch units as more force is needed (all-or-nothing contraction at the individual fiber level).

## Reflexes
- **Myotatic stretch reflex**: triggered by the muscle spindle (a proprioceptor sensing length/rate of change) when a muscle is stretched too fast/far — causes reflexive contraction of the same muscle to prevent overstretching injury.
- **Golgi tendon organ (GTO) / tension reflex**: proprioceptor at the muscle-tendon junction; senses excess tension and reflexively reduces further contraction to protect the tendon from tearing. Can be trained (its threshold rises with repeated exercise, allowing more strength) but overtraining risks tendon avulsion.

## Types of contractions
- **Static (isometric)**: muscle contracts, joint doesn't move (e.g. holding a door open).
- **Dynamic**: joint moves. Includes concentric (muscle shortens while contracting — "movement producers") and eccentric (muscle lengthens while contracting — "movement stoppers," controls motion, e.g. triceps controlling elbow flexion done by biceps).
- **Muscle roles in a movement**: agonist/prime mover (main muscle producing the movement), assistor (secondary contributor), antagonist (opposes the movement, usually relaxing eccentrically to allow it), stabilizer (fixes a body part so force can be exerted, e.g. core muscles during a soccer kick), neutralizer (cancels unwanted side-effects of other contracting muscles to keep the movement on-target).

## Force-velocity relationship & training (pages 52-59)
- **Force-velocity relationship**: more velocity = less force, and vice versa (throwing a baseball fast vs. a heavy shot put slowly). As resistance grows, a concentric contraction transitions to isometric (e.g. stuck mid-dumbbell-curl) then to eccentric as load exceeds the muscle's force output — eccentric contractions can generate more force than concentric ones, and force keeps rising with velocity in the eccentric direction.
- **Length-tension relationship**: a muscle produces max force at ~120-130% of resting length (slightly stretched) — e.g. squatting before jumping to touch a hoop.
- **Electromechanical delay**: the transition time from eccentric to concentric firing (aka stretch-shortening cycle / amortization time). Too long a delay loses the stored elastic energy; too short doesn't allow full force development. Plyometric training (rapid stretch then forceful contraction, e.g. jump training) exploits this cycle for more power. Long electromechanical delays are linked to certain injuries (e.g. some shoulder injuries).
- **Specificity of training**: adaptations are specific to (a) the muscle fibers used at a given training intensity — training slow-twitch endurance won't prepare fast-twitch fibers needed for a race-ending sprint kick; (b) the speed trained at — strength gained slowly doesn't transfer to speed; (c) the joint angle/body position trained; and (d) the muscle fiber chemistry (aerobic vs. anaerobic enzyme systems) — train the specific adaptation you want.
- **Changing the load changes your speed**: training with a load similar to your sport's actual load is important — e.g. training with an overly heavy bat can actually slow your normal bat swing.
- **Strength training rules for hypertrophy**:
  1. **Lift heavy enough** — need loads ≥60% of your one-repetition max (1RM) to cause the stress/damage that drives adaptation.
  2. **Lift to fatigue** — submaximal loads only recruit all fibers once earlier-recruited ones fatigue and the body taps additional fibers; don't stop early.
  3. **Growth happens during recovery** — eat enough carbs (50-60% of diet) and protein (0.4-0.6 g/lb bodyweight) to fuel/build muscle; growth hormone and testosterone peak during sleep; recovery time (24-48+ hrs) should scale with training intensity/age.
  4. **Progressively increase the load** — once a muscle adapts to a load (e.g. hitting 15-20 reps easily), increase load by ~10% to keep driving adaptation.
- **Adaptations to strength training**: nervous-system adaptations happen fast (within 4-8 weeks) — increased recruitment of available fibers, increased neural firing frequency, increased motor-unit synchronization, and increased Golgi tendon organ activation threshold (more force tolerated before the tendon-protection reflex kicks in). Muscle-tissue adaptations take longer (8-12+ weeks) — hypertrophy (fast-twitch fibers respond most), increased contractile proteins (more actin/myosin), and a shift of fast-twitch X fibers toward fast-twitch A characteristics (strength gained at some cost to endurance).
- **Muscle fatigue sources**: (1) running out of fuel — creatine phosphate lasts ~10 seconds of high-intensity work; glycogen (~2,000 calories stored in liver/muscle) lasts roughly a 20-mile run; (2) lactic acid — its H+ ion competes with calcium for the troponin binding site, blocking further contraction (felt as a "choppy" stride/pedal rate) and also slows nerve conduction along axons, throwing off movement coordination/timing; (3) the brain itself limits effort by sending fatigue/pain signals via reduced motor output and sensory feedback, protecting the body from injury from overexertion (e.g. why a runner "hits the wall" before truly running out of fuel).

## Chapter 4: Keeping the Big Wheel Turning — Exercise Metabolism (pages 63-77 of ~91; partial)
Three primary ATP-production systems, providing energy at different rates:
- **Phosphocreatine (ATP-PC) system**: immediate energy, lasts only ~8-10 seconds of all-out effort (sprints, jumps, one heavy lift). Phosphocreatine donates a phosphate to ADP (via creatine kinase) to instantly remake ATP. Creatine supplementation may modestly boost high-intensity performance for some people (mixed results; minor side effects like water retention/weight gain, which can hurt speed).
- **Anaerobic glycolysis**: fast energy without oxygen, sustains effort for up to ~5 minutes. Breaks down glucose (from blood or muscle/liver glycogen) in two steps: (1) invests 2 ATP to trap/prime glucose into fructose 6-phosphate, (2) the molecule splits and produces 4 ATP — net gain of 2 ATP per glucose. End product is pyruvic acid, converted to lactic acid via the enzyme LDH to slow acid buildup — but lactic acid itself becomes the fatigue culprit: its H+ ions (1) block muscle contraction by competing with calcium for troponin binding sites, (2) slow nerve signal conduction, (3) inhibit the glycolysis enzyme phosphofructokinase (capping ATP production), and (4) directly cause pain, which reduces motor effort. Fatigue from lactic acid occurs both centrally (brain) and peripherally (muscle/cell). Recovery: breathing off lactic acid via blood bicarbonate (HCO3- + H+ → carbonic acid → CO2 + H2O, exhaled) and using slow-twitch fibers (light walking/jogging) to metabolize lactic acid faster. Glycogen supercompensation ("carb-loading": taper training + raise carb intake to 60-70%+ of diet in the days before a big event) maximizes stored glycogen for endurance events.
- **Oxidative (aerobic) system**: slower but sustains effort indefinitely (a full lifetime), limited only by fuel/oxygen supply. Occurs in mitochondria ("nature's batteries" — outer/inner membrane, matrix, mitochondrial DNA), which are more abundant in aerobic (slow-twitch) fibers. Uses glucose, fat, and (minimally, ~5-10%) protein as fuel: both glucose (via pyruvate) and fat (via beta-oxidation, breaking fat's carbon chain into 2-carbon units) convert into acetyl CoA, which enters the **Krebs cycle** — a repeating series of molecular rearrangements (citrate → isocitrate → alpha-ketoglutarate → succinyl CoA → succinate → fumarate → malate → oxaloacetate) that releases H+ ions and CO2 (waste, exhaled) each turn. B vitamins niacin and riboflavin form NAD/FAD carriers that shuttle these H+ ions to the **electron transport system (ETS)**, on the mitochondria's inner membrane, which uses the H+ ions (like a battery's electrical gradient) via the enzyme ATPase to reattach phosphate to ADP and produce ATP. Finally, oxygen combines with leftover H+ to form water (2H+ + O → H2O) — oxygen's key role is neutralizing/removing H+ so it doesn't cause the same fatigue effects seen in anaerobic glycolysis. Protein, when used for energy, either feeds into the Krebs cycle directly (as amino acids) or gets converted to glucose via gluconeogenesis when carbs are low (at the cost of breaking down muscle).

## Measuring metabolism: VO2 (pages 78-89)
- **Calorie/kilocalorie**: a calorie is the heat needed to raise 1 mL of water 1°C; a kilocalorie (what "Calories" on food labels mean) is 1,000x that, for 1 liter of water. About 80% of ATP's energy becomes heat (not muscle work) — why exercise makes you sweat and shivering warms you.
- **VO2 (volume of oxygen consumed)**: measured in L/min (rest ~0.25 L/min, peak up to 6.0 L/min); **VO2 max** = the ceiling of oxygen use at maximal effort, the standard fitness benchmark. Rule of thumb: 1 liter of oxygen consumed ≈ 5 kilocalories burned (calories = VO2 L/min × 5).
- **Comparing fitness fairly**: raw VO2 max isn't a fair comparison across body sizes — convert to mL/kg/min (weight in lbs ÷ 2.2 = kg; VO2 L/min × 1000 = mL/min; divide by kg). Fitness category tables exist by sex (e.g. men: <45 low, 46-55 average, 56+ high; women: <40 low, 41-49 average, 50+ high).
- **Estimating calories burned from an activity**: VO2 (mL/kg/min) = (speed in meters/min × 0.1) + 3.5 for walking, then convert to L/min using body weight, then × 5 for kcal/min.
- **Anaerobic threshold (AT)**: the exercise intensity where lactic acid suddenly spikes as fast-twitch/anaerobic fibers kick in alongside slow-twitch/aerobic ones. At low intensity, aerobic fibers + Krebs cycle dominate (no lactic acid, fat+carbs both used); at moderate intensity, fast-twitch fibers start contributing and lactic acid begins accumulating; at high intensity, nearly all fibers fire, fat use drops, glucose becomes primary fuel, and lactic acid production is heavy. Athletes race just below their AT for best sustainable performance. AT can be identified via blood lactate sampling or non-invasively via ventilation/CO2 changes (e.g. a runner's AT at 8.0 mph / 7.5 min/mile pace can predict their 10K time: 6.2 miles × pace).
- **Training for improved metabolism = training enzymes**: more enzymes = faster ATP production, and training is enzyme-specific:
  - **ATP-phosphocreatine system**: 5-10 second high-intensity intervals with short rest, using competition-specific muscles → faster phosphocreatine breakdown/storage, better all-out effort.
  - **Anaerobic glycolytic system**: 30-60 second high-intensity intervals with 1-5 min light-activity rest → more enzymes/ATP, less lactic acid at a given speed, better oxygen delivery, more mitochondria, bigger heart/blood delivery.
  - **Oxidative (aerobic) system**: moderate-intensity training for general aerobic fitness (even 2x/week helps if starting unfit); high-intensity training can additionally improve the glycolytic system as a bonus. Best activities use large muscle groups (walking, jogging, swimming, hiking, biking, rowing).

## Chapter 5: The Body's Engine — The Cardiovascular System
- **Heart structure**: a muscular pump with 4 chambers (2 atria, 2 ventricles) and valves; right side handles deoxygenated blood (returns from body → lungs), left side handles oxygenated blood (from lungs → out to body). Heart sounds ("lub-dub") come from valves closing; murmurs indicate abnormal valve function.
- **Blood flow path**: deoxygenated blood enters right atrium → right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs (gas exchange) → pulmonary vein → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → body. Coronary arteries supply the heart muscle itself.
- **Cardiac cycle**: systole (contraction, ejecting blood) and diastole (relaxation, filling with blood).
- **Blood pressure**: systolic pressure (arterial pressure during systole/contraction) over diastolic pressure (during diastole/rest). Chronic high blood pressure (hypertension) damages arteries over time.
- **Heart rate control**: the sinoatrial (SA) node is the heart's natural pacemaker, generating the electrical signal that spreads through the atria, to the atrioventricular (AV) node, then through ventricular conduction tissue, triggering coordinated contraction.
- **Key measures of heart function**: heart rate (beats/min), stroke volume (blood pumped per beat), cardiac output (heart rate × stroke volume = blood volume pumped per minute).
- **Delivering oxygen to cells**: air is ~21% oxygen; exhaled air is ~16% (the ~5% difference was used by mitochondria). Gases move via partial pressure gradients — oxygen/CO2 diffuse from areas of high to low partial pressure between lungs, blood, and tissues. Hemoglobin in red blood cells carries the vast majority of oxygen in blood (a small amount dissolves directly in plasma).
- **Blood vessel control**: vessels can open (vasodilate) or close (vasoconstrict) to direct blood flow; factors include local tissue chemistry (heat, CO2, acidity opens vessels near working muscle) and nervous system signals. Chemoreceptors sense blood chemistry (O2, CO2, acid) and baroreceptors sense blood pressure, feeding back to the brain's cardiovascular control center.
- **Effects of exercise on the cardiovascular system**: acute adjustments — heart rate, stroke volume, cardiac output, and blood pressure all rise with exercise intensity to meet working muscles' oxygen demand; a "steady state" is reached a few minutes into moderate exercise. Long-term (chronic) adaptations from aerobic training — increased stroke volume and cardiac output, resting heart rate drops (a well-conditioned heart pumps more blood per beat, needing fewer beats/minute), and the heart muscle itself strengthens. Strength training also drives acute cardiovascular adjustments (e.g. blood pressure spikes during a heavy lift, "the lift").

## Chapter 6: Earthlings and the Earth — Adapting to Your Environment
- **Thermoregulation**: the hypothalamus acts as the body's internal thermostat, receiving temperature signals and triggering heat-gain or heat-loss mechanisms to keep core temperature stable.
- **Heat gain mechanisms**: radiation, conduction, convection, and metabolic heat production (muscle activity always produces heat as a byproduct, as covered in Ch.4).
- **Heat loss / cooling mechanisms**: sweating (evaporative cooling — most effective, but humidity reduces its efficiency since sweat must evaporate, not just drip, to cool the body), vasodilation of skin blood vessels (radiates heat), and behavioral responses.
- **Exercising in heat**: increases risk of dehydration and heat illness. Progression of heat injury: heat cramps (mildest) → heat exhaustion (heavy sweating, dizziness, nausea) → heat stroke (most severe — body temperature regulation fails, medical emergency). Fluid replacement guidance: weigh yourself before/after exercise to estimate fluid lost as sweat, replace with water/sports drinks. Acclimating to heat takes about 1-2 weeks of repeated heat exposure, after which sweat rate and cardiovascular efficiency improve.
- **Exercising in cold**: hypothermia (dangerously low core body temperature) and wind chill are key risks. Wet or wind-exposed skin loses heat faster. Layered clothing (moisture-wicking base layer, insulating mid-layer, wind/water-resistant outer layer) helps regulate temperature; overdressing risks overheating/sweating, which then increases cold risk once activity stops.
- **Exercising at altitude**: reduced atmospheric oxygen pressure at altitude means less oxygen reaches the blood despite normal breathing — reduces aerobic capacity/performance. Altitude illnesses include acute mountain sickness, high-altitude pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs), and high-altitude cerebral edema (fluid in brain, most severe). Acclimatization (increased red blood cell/hemoglobin production, ventilation adaptations) takes days to weeks. "Live high, train low" is a training strategy elite endurance athletes use — live at altitude for the blood adaptations (more oxygen-carrying capacity) but train at lower altitude where more oxygen is available for higher-quality workouts.

## Chapter 7: Planning Your Movements — Motor Control
- **Neuron structure**: dendrites (receive signals) → cell body → axon (sends signals); sensory neurons carry info to the CNS, motor neurons carry commands to muscles, interneurons connect the two within the CNS. Neurotransmitters carry signals across synapses.
- **Brain regions**: cerebral cortex (higher thought/planning), sensory cortex and motor cortex (process sensation, initiate voluntary movement), cerebellum (coordination/balance/fine-tuning movement), basal ganglia (movement initiation/regulation — Parkinson's disease results from basal ganglia dopamine loss, causing tremor/rigidity/slowed movement), brain stem (basic life functions, relay between brain and spinal cord).
- **Spinal cord**: relays signals between brain and body; enables fast reflexes without waiting on the brain.
- **Feedback loops**: open-loop systems execute a pre-planned movement without real-time sensory correction (fast, ballistic movements); closed-loop systems continuously use sensory feedback to adjust movement in progress (slower, more precise).
- **Sensory information**: touch, temperature, pressure, vision, and proprioception (sense of body position/movement without looking) all feed motor control.
- **Muscle spindles & Golgi tendon organs**: muscle spindles detect stretch/length change and trigger the stretch reflex (protective contraction against overstretching); Golgi tendon organs detect tension and trigger the tension reflex (protective relaxation against excessive force, e.g. autogenic inhibition). Plyometric training exploits the stretch-shortening cycle for explosive power.
- **Speed-accuracy tradeoff**: faster movements are generally less accurate; slowing down improves precision.
- **Movement phases**: preparation, initiation, and termination.
- **Bimanual coordination**: the two hands/arms working together, often with a temporal coupling between them.
- **Gait/locomotion**: walking cycle has a stance phase (contact/heel strike → midstance → propulsion/toe-off) and a swing phase (leg swings forward after toe-off to the next heel strike). Parkinson's disease disrupts normal gait patterns (shuffling, reduced arm swing). Trunk movement contributes to balance and efficiency during gait.

## Chapter 8: The Nuts and Bolts of Movement — Biomechanics
- **Biomechanics** is the study of forces and their effects on the human body during movement. A biomechanist's problem-solving process: understand the task/objective, observe and collect data, evaluate/diagnose (identify inefficiencies or injury risk), and share findings/recommendations.
- **Worked example — "How Do I Run Faster?"**: analyzing the gait cycle (stance and swing phases) to spot inefficiencies like overstriding or excessive bounce, and recommending stride/form adjustments.
- **Forms of motion**: linear motion (rectilinear — straight line, e.g. a train; curvilinear — uniform curved path, e.g. a car jumping a ramp), angular motion (rotation around an axis — e.g. a limb at a joint, a figure skater spinning), and general motion (a mix of linear + angular, e.g. a thrown ball or a person walking).
- **Anatomical position & directional terms**: standing upright, feet shoulder-width apart, arms at sides, palms forward. Superior/inferior, anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal, superficial/deep, axial.
- **Planes of motion**: sagittal (divides body left/right), transverse (divides top/bottom), frontal/coronal (divides front/back) — movement is described relative to these planes and their corresponding axes of rotation (anteroposterior, mediolateral, vertical/longitudinal).
- **Joint motions by plane**:
  - Sagittal plane: flexion (decreases joint angle), extension (increases it), hyperextension (past anatomical position), dorsiflexion/plantar flexion (ankle-specific).
  - Frontal plane: abduction (away from midline), adduction (toward midline) — also elevation/depression (shoulder), lateral flexion (spine), inversion/eversion (foot).
  - Transverse plane: internal (medial) rotation and external (lateral) rotation, plus supination/pronation (forearm).
  - Other: diagonal motion (crosses multiple planes, e.g. throwing) and circumduction (a limb tracing a cone-shaped path).
- **Levers**: a rigid segment rotating about a fulcrum (axis), driven by force (effort) against resistance. In the body, bones are levers, joints are fulcrums, and muscle contraction supplies force.
  - First-class lever: fulcrum between force and resistance (e.g. seesaw; the head/neck at the atlanto-occipital joint).
  - Second-class lever: resistance between fulcrum and force (e.g. wheelbarrow; standing on tiptoes — the ball of the foot is the fulcrum).
  - Third-class lever: force between fulcrum and resistance — the most common lever type in the body, favors speed/range of motion over force (e.g. a dumbbell bicep curl).
  - **Force arm vs. resistance arm**: force needed is inversely proportional to the force arm's length — longer force arm = less force needed, but at a mechanical/speed disadvantage. Most body levers (e.g. the elbow) have a short force arm relative to resistance arm, prioritizing speed/range over raw strength.
  - **Velocity**: longer lever arms impart more velocity to an object (e.g. a longer baseball bat) but take longer to swing — a speed/time-to-completion tradeoff. Shortening the lever arm (bending the elbow) lets a thrower snap the arm faster.
- **Balance, equilibrium, and stability**: equilibrium is zero net change in the body's speed/direction; balance is the ability to control equilibrium (static or dynamic); stability is resistance to a change in the body's equilibrium. Joint ligaments (e.g. the knee's medial collateral ligament) constrain motion to protect stability; being "double-jointed" just means unusually flexible joints/ligaments, not truly dislocated joints.
- **Kinematics**: describes motion (displacement, distance) without regard to the forces causing it — qualitative (descriptive) or quantitative (measured, e.g. via high-speed camera/motion capture). Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to end point; distance is the total path length traveled (they can differ significantly, e.g. a punt returner weaving downfield).
- **Kinetics**: the study of forces and their effects on mechanical systems (the "why" behind movement, complementing kinematics' "what"). Key concepts: inertia, mass, force, center of gravity, weight, torque, impulse.
  - Inertia: resistance to a change in motion state; proportional to mass.
  - Mass vs. weight: mass is the amount of matter in an object (constant); weight is the gravitational force on that mass (varies by location, e.g. the moon).
  - Force = mass × acceleration.
  - Center of gravity: the point where an object's mass is balanced; the reference point for analyzing rotational motion (e.g. a gymnast's center of gravity).
  - Torque: the turning effect of an eccentric force (not aligned with the axis of rotation) — more torque = more rotation.
  - Impulse: force applied over time; the same force delivered briefly (kicking a medicine ball) causes more pain/injury than delivered gradually (pushing it slowly).
  - Velocity and acceleration: velocity is speed + direction; acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.
- **Mechanical loads** — types of force delivered to the body: compression (squeezing along an axis — the body compresses slightly over the course of a day due to gravity), tension (pulling, causing bone bumps/tuberosities where muscles attach), shear (force perpendicular to an object's long axis, can cause fractures), bending (compression on one side + tension on the other), torsion (twisting about the long axis with one end fixed — common in sports injuries), and combined loading (multiple load types at once — the most common real-world case).
- **Newton's laws of motion** applied to the body:
  - First law (inertia): a body at rest/motion stays that way unless acted on by an outside force.
  - Second law (acceleration): acceleration is proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass — a lighter object is easier to move/accelerate.
  - Third law (action-reaction): every force has an equal and opposite reaction force (e.g. pushing off the ground to jump; an arm-wrestling contest).
- **Measuring kinetics**: choosing the right force/variable to measure depends on the question (e.g. muscle activity via EMG, gait via force plates); kinetic measurement equipment is often expensive and requires trained operators.
- **Concussions sidebar**: growing research (notably in NFL) on head-impact forces; helmets with force transducers/accelerometers now measure impact magnitude/direction and can alert to potential concussion risk in real time (e.g. youth ski helmets messaging parents' phones), aiding faster follow-up care.

## Chapter 9: These Joints Are A-Jumping
- **Why joints matter**: joints let bones bend/twist/glide; without them, structural support alone wouldn't allow movement. Flexibility and habitual movement patterns differ by person and occupation, affecting what tasks a body can do.

### Types of joints
- **Structural classification** (by connecting tissue):
  - *Fibrous*: connected by fibrous tissue, slightly movable (e.g. lower arm's radio-ulnar joint).
  - *Cartilaginous*: connected by cartilage — hyaline cartilage (covers synovial joint surfaces, reduces friction) or fibrocartilage (spongy, shock-absorbing, e.g. between vertebrae). Pubic symphysis and vertebrae are cartilaginous joint examples.
  - *Synovial*: most common type — hyaline cartilage covering plus a synovial cavity/capsule with lubricating synovial fluid. Greatest range of motion (elbow, wrist, hip, shoulder).
- **Functional classification** (by movement allowed):
  - *Synarthroses* (immovable): sutures (skull bones), syndesmoses (dense fibrous tissue joints, e.g. radio-ulnar, tibio-fibular).
  - *Amphiarthroses* (slightly movable): connected by fibrocartilage/hyaline cartilage, e.g. intervertebral disks, pubic symphysis, sternoclavicular joint.
  - *Diarthroses* (freely movable, = synovial joints): the most common type, joined by ligaments.
- **Table 9-1 — Types of Diarthrotic Joints**: ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip — circular, all planes/rotation), ellipsoid (knuckles — multiple planes, no rotation), gliding (carpal/tarsal bones — sliding/twisting), hinge (elbow, knee — flexion/extension only), pivot (radio-ulnar, atlanto-axial — rotation only), saddle (carpometacarpal of thumb — many planes, no rotation).
- **Degrees of freedom**: joints classified by axes of rotation allowed — uniaxial (1), biaxial (2, e.g. ellipsoid knuckle joint: flexion/extension + abduction/adduction), or tri-axial (3, e.g. ball-and-socket).

### Joint stability & connective tissue
- **Articular cartilage** (hyaline): thin, dense covering that distributes load and reduces friction between articulating bones.
- **Articular fibrocartilage** (menisci/intervertebral disks): distributes load, improves fit, limits slip, protects edges, lubricates, absorbs shock.
- **Tendon**: connects muscle to bone; overstress causes tendonitis.
- **Ligament**: connects bone to bone; injury/overstretching causes sprains.
- **Joint capsule**: dense connective tissue surrounding the joint, works with ligaments/cartilage to reduce friction during movement.
- Tendons/ligaments are non-contractile but extensible — they stretch under load and return to normal length, but stretched beyond their elastic limit they become damaged/loose (requiring surgery to fully correct) and joint function is compromised.

### Factors affecting stability
- **Shape/contact points**: convex-concave bone shapes (ball-and-socket) increase stability. Closed-packed position = maximum bone-surface contact (most stable); loose-packed/open-packed position = less contact, less stable.
- **Ligament integrity**: ligaments resist tension and keep bones together; injury/imbalance reduces stability.
- **Muscle balance around a joint**: e.g. quads (extensors) are typically stronger than hamstrings (flexors) at the knee — a muscle imbalance here can destabilize the joint and lead to injury; muscles must be strengthened in a functionally balanced way.
- **Muscle/tendon length**: e.g. tight hamstrings limiting knee extension/heel-strike during gait, affecting overall movement integration.
- Ligaments/tendons can atrophy (shrink) or hypertrophy (grow) over time depending on demand; prior joint injury (e.g. a sprained ankle) leaves looser ligaments and higher re-injury risk.
- **ACL injury sidebar**: very common in athletes; the ACL prevents the tibia from sliding too far forward on the femur, often injured decelerating/changing direction quickly (non-contact injury). Risk factors: quad/hamstring strength imbalance (hamstrings ideally within ~1/3 of quad strength), Q-angle (knock-knees), femoral condyle/intercondylar notch shape, and possibly hormonal changes during menstruation — evidence on hormonal link is not yet strong. Rehabilitatable with time/work, not career-ending.

### Restraint mechanisms for joint stability
- **Active restraint mechanism**: the muscles — contractile, provide coordinated force/resistance to protect the joint (e.g. all the muscles needed to turn a doorknob working together). Requires working in a coordinated way, receiving/interpreting sensory information in a timely manner, and reacting with adequate force.
- **Passive restraint mechanism**: non-contractile structures — cartilage, bones, ligaments, connective tissues, plus mechanoreceptors (specialized sensory receptors detecting tension/pressure/movement and feeding back to the CNS to trigger reflexes or motor plans, e.g. reflexively pulling a hand off a hot surface).

### Flexibility
- **Static flexibility**: amount of motion at the end of a joint's range, held still (e.g. touching toes and holding). Assessed via the "envelope of motion" from a relaxed starting position.
- **Dynamic flexibility**: motion during actual movement across multiple joints simultaneously (a "dynamic task"), e.g. throwing a ball involves elbow, shoulder, wrist, spine, and legs together. A limitation at one joint often causes compensation elsewhere (e.g. limited ankle dorsiflexion during gait forces early heel-off).
- **Range of motion (ROM)**: measured in degrees from anatomical position (zero/neutral). Measured with a goniometer (measures joint angles, 360° or 180° models) or an inclinometer (strapped to limb).
- **End feel**: how a joint feels when it reaches the terminal point of its ROM —
  - *Bone-to-bone*: abrupt, hard stop (e.g. elbow extension, olecranon process meeting the humerus).
  - *Soft-tissue approximation*: stop caused by soft tissue compression (e.g. elbow flexion, biceps compressing against forearm).
  - *Tissue stretch*: gradual increasing resistance/tension as tissue stretches (e.g. bending fingers back toward the forearm).
  - Abnormal end feel vs. what's anatomically expected suggests injury (e.g. torn ligament).
- **Flexibility vs. laxity sidebar**: flexibility = normal ROM within a joint; laxity = looseness/excess translation between bones, which can be either benign or destabilizing/injury-prone (e.g. shoulder rotating too far causing instability, or in extreme cases dislocation). Strengthening surrounding musculature counters excess laxity.

### Stretching
- **Autogenic inhibition**: when a muscle is stretched, muscle spindles first react by contracting to prevent overstretch; after ~10 seconds held, Golgi tendon organs (GTOs) override this and signal relaxation, allowing further stretch — this GTO-driven relaxation response is what makes progressive stretching possible.
- **Reciprocal inhibition**: contracting the agonist muscle (e.g. quads) causes reflexive relaxation of the antagonist (hamstrings) via GTO signaling, allowing more efficient movement/stretch of the opposing muscle group.
- **Collagen, elastin, actin, myosin**: noncontractile tissue components (collagen resists force/manipulation, elastin returns tissue to original shape) and contractile components (actin/myosin, the myofilaments from Ch.3 responsible for contraction) both determine a structure's ability to stretch and recover; permanent lengthening of muscle/tendon requires long-term repeated stretching, and is difficult to achieve safely.
- **Agonist/antagonist balance**: agonist causes movement (contracts), antagonist is stretched by that movement (e.g. biceps = agonist, triceps = antagonist during elbow flexion); imbalance between the two increases injury risk.
- **Stretching techniques**:
  - *Active stretching*: contracting the agonist to move the joint and stretch the antagonist, no external help.
  - *Passive stretching*: gravity, body weight, or a partner moves the joint through its ROM to the point of stretch.
  - *Static stretching*: most common — hold a stretch position (often ~5-60 seconds) via gravity/body weight/a partner.
  - *Ballistic stretching*: repetitive bouncing movements to increase ROM — controversial (injury risk debate) but widely used; care must be taken that bouncing intensity doesn't create undue joint/muscle stress.
  - *Dynamic stretching*: active, controlled stretching that mimics the actual activity being prepared for (e.g. throwing motion warm-up) — increasingly popular in sport, both loosens and warms muscles.
  - *Proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF)*: advanced technique combining agonist/antagonist contraction-relaxation cycles (typically 3 reps, each phase ~10 seconds), maximizing the GTO response for flexibility gains. Requires care — don't push too hard, don't transition too quickly between contracting/relaxing.
    - *Hold-relax PNF*: isometric (no-movement) contraction of the stretched (antagonist) muscle at point of stretch, then pushed further after releasing.
    - *Contract-relax PNF*: isotonic (movement) contraction of the antagonist against partner resistance, then returned and pushed to a new stretch point.
    - *Slow-reversal-hold-relax PNF*: a 5-step partner-assisted sequence alternating active stretch-back, agonist contraction to intensify stretch, and rest, repeated from the new position — the most coordination-intensive PNF variant.
- **Practical notes on stretching**: increasing ROM isn't always the actual goal — sometimes just relaxing/loosening tissue relieves pain without ROM change (e.g. runner's knee from a tight IT band: loosening it relieves pain without needing more knee ROM). Desired results can sometimes come from stretching areas other than the painful joint itself, since the body's joints/muscles are interconnected (e.g. stretching the hip to relieve knee pain). Chapter closes with the mantra: **"Move in all ways and always move!"**
