# Isaac Newton: The Asshole Who Reinvented the Universe

# Isaac Newton: The Asshole Who Reinvented the Universe — Introduction & Chapter 1

*A popular-science/biography that takes Newton's unpleasant personality seriously as a lens on how science actually gets done. The author is an astronomer who repeatedly notes his own work depends on Newton's foundations.*

---

## INTRODUCTION (pp. 11–15)

**Core thesis:** Isaac Newton was both a towering genius — the man who almost single-handedly founded modern physical science — **and** a selfish, belligerent, unfriendly, argumentative, secretive bully and religious fanatic. The book argues these two facts are not in tension; the most interesting question is whether he could have changed the world the way he did *without* being the way he was.

### Key points
- **Birth date ambiguity:** Born Christmas Day 1642 (Julian calendar) *or* January 4, 1643 (Gregorian) — depends on which calendar you consult. Two calendars were in use at the time.
- The author worships Newton's science while admitting Newton was "an asshole to boot." He would not want Newton as a colleague — Newton would "delight in making enemies his whole life long and yet still manage to revolutionize the world of science."
- **Provocative question:** Can a genius and asshole still be a role model for a successful scientist today? The book treats this seriously rather than rhetorically.
- Newton's science *survived* — his laws of physics remain valid (within their domain) and continue to be confirmed; this is what makes biography of him worthwhile, since few scientists three centuries on are still read as "current."
- **Childhood evidence of character:** As a boy Newton kept a notebook listing his own *sins*. Entries reveal a difficult, antisocial child:
  - "Threatening my father and mother... to burn them and the house over them"
  - "Striking many," "Wishing death and hoping it to some," "Stealing cherry cobs," "Making pies on Sunday night," "Making a mousetrap on Sunday"
  - The list of sins itself heightens our sense of his isolation.
- Newton preferred building things (water mills, machines, sundials accurate enough to tell time from sunlight on his wall) and observing the physical world over playing with other children.
- **Farming failure:** Pulled out of school at sixteen by his widowed mother to run the family farm; he was hopeless and uninterested at it — "above all a difficult, selfish, and childish young man" with no desire for manual labor. Fortunately a former schoolteacher and his uncle convinced his mother to send him to the **University of Cambridge**.
- **June 1661:** Newton left his home village of **Woolsthorpe**, Lincolnshire, for Cambridge, where he began the work that would "shake the world." The author's verdict, in hindsight: *"What a scientific genius! And what an asshole!"*

## CHAPTER 1 — At All Costs: Newton, Ruthless in the Extreme (pp. 18–40)

Opening frame: Even today scientists are stereotyped as an odd, socially isolated, badly dressed bunch incapable of small talk, locked away in their labs. Newton took every one of these traits **to the extreme**. He was "the epitome of a nerd," and the chapter explores his uncompromising, socially oblivious, ruthless pursuit of knowledge — what the author calls his "asshole territory."

### Humphry Newton's testimony (the assistant)
**Humphry Newton** (no relation), Newton's Cambridge assistant, recorded that Newton:
- Never took recreation or a pastime — "Thinking all Hours lost that was not spent in his Studies."
- So absorbed he ate sparingly, often forgetting to eat at all; food going into his chamber would come out untouched.
- Rarely went to bed before 2 or 3 a.m.; sometimes not until 5 or 6, sleeping only 4–5 hours.
- Sometimes went to the dining Hall with shoes down at heels, stockings untied, surplice on, and hair "scarcely combed."
- When Newton did go to a quick meal, a sudden thought would send him hurrying back to his study to work — food "too precious to be wasted on the act of sitting down."
- **No records survive of students attending his lectures** — they were "extremely limited," sometimes delivered to an empty hall because "few went to hear Him, & fewer understood him." He would lecture to the walls rather than cancel.
- The only other interest: occasionally walking in his vegetable garden, where he would compulsively pull out weeds.

> Theme: little sleep, eating on his feet, lectures to empty halls, a distaste for weeds — Newton "being a harmless figure of fun." But his rigor "knew no bounds." His own recipe for a successful scientific career: *"Fresh air, fasting and little wine."* He admitted to "too much study (whence... cometh madness)."

### "Talking Dogs and Vomiting Salamanders" (the 17th-century scientific context)
To understand Newton, the author stresses how little separated "natural science" from superstition in the 1600s. Nature was full of unanswered mystery, and serious scholarly journals printed credulous, bizarre reports as genuine science:

| Source / report | Claim treated as scientific fact |
|---|---|
| *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society* (founded 1665, first issue March 1665 — oldest continuously published science journal) | Vehicle for the era's leading scholars, but also for absurd reports |
| Mr. Colepresse, "A Relation of an uncommon accident in two aged persons" | Joseph Shute (~70) and Maria Stert (~90) reportedly **grew new teeth** in advanced age — treated as remarkable but real |
| Letter "from Rome" | A **salamander living in fire** that, pulled from overseas into a fire to extinguish flames, **vomited continuously for two hours** — the journal hedged with "hazard," "report puts it," "research reports" |
| Report from Syria | A snake bit England-bound fish, which fell from the heavens with two heads — preserved and displayed |
| *Philosophical Transactions*, "An extract of M. Dela Quintiny's Letter" (in French) | A method for **ordering melons** so they ripen on schedule — four whole pages with diagrams on how melons should be arranged, trimmed, and harvested |
| **Leibniz** (the eminent mathematician/philosopher, Newton's rival — see later chapters) | Wrote of a **talking dog** in Zeitz, Saxony-Anhalt: a child taught it to say ~30 words (e.g., *thé*, *caffé*, *chocolat*) — Leibniz took this seriously |
| Robert Boyle / Robert Hooke ("Renowned scientists") | Among the era's giants, yet also reported the credulous and curious |
| A goat with an "extremely unusual haircut" (Zwickau, at a Herr Winckel's home) | First reported as completely normal hair until kicked by a passerby, after which it grew a strange hairstyle; Leibniz speculated the goat's grief over its lost coat changed its hair |
| Calf born in Saxony | Deformed skull called "Head of a Colt"; Leibniz wrote articles on it for the *Journal des sçavans* (one of the oldest scholarly journals) |

The point: in a world where "deformed baby cows, fire-extinguishing salamanders" were treated as legitimate science, the people — **like Newton** — busy trying to *actually* understand the world and develop true scientific methods were the exception. What we call philosophy/proto-science of the time did contain real seeds, but it would take centuries to become genuine natural science.

### "Research, Regardless of the Consequences" (Newton sticks a needle in his eye)
A famous, gruesome example of Newton's ruthlessness *toward himself*:
- To investigate the nature of vision and color perception, Newton **stuck a large blunt needle (a "bodkin") between his eyeball and the bone of the socket**, pressed against the back of the eye, and observed the colored circles and changes in the appearance of light it produced — testing whether visual sensations arose from pressure rather than incoming light.
- He also **stared at the Sun** (reflected, then directly) during/around a solar eclipse for as long as he could bear, to study afterimages and colors — then locked himself in a completely darkened room for **three days** until his vision recovered.
- He nearly blinded himself. The lesson the author draws: Newton applied to himself the same total disregard for sensitivity, comfort, and safety that he applied to everyone else. His thirst for knowledge "knew no bounds" — he was "definitely not a softie."
- Context: this self-experiment was less crazy then than now. Ancient Greek philosophers (Euclid, Ptolemy) thought vision worked by rays *projecting out of the eyes*; Newton was probing how the eye perceives the world — a fair and important question. "Sticking a needle in your own eye" is on the list of things he did to chase an answer.

### Newton vs. his contemporaries (the social cost)
- Newton was "completely understandable" in being annoyed by the credulous, ordinary thinking of his contemporaries — he took a radically different, rigorous approach and was "absolutely convinced" his critics had no idea what they were discussing.
- But he was **not an understanding man**: he refused to take other people's views into account, would not participate in academic debate, was offended that others didn't appreciate his methods, and made no effort to meet anyone halfway. His scientific ideas had genuine value, yet his refusal to engage cost him allies. Many scientists of the century "still had to get used to this idea" of true rigor.

### THE WARDEN AND THE FORGER (pp. 29–37)
A jump from the young, unknown Newton to the old, famous one — by now an uncompromising, "famous scientist and president of the Royal Society," even more so than in his youth. Far from retiring, in **1696** Newton left Cambridge for London and took the post of **Warden of the Royal Mint** (later Master). He was, in effect, "an early superhero complete with a cloak and supernatural powers" — armed with a real official title and real power to fight crime.

**Why he left Cambridge:** His life there had split between Woolsthorpe and Cambridge; academia had become dull and offered no new challenges or evidence of activity. He had also sat in the **English Parliament** (1689 and 1690) where, famously, his only recorded contribution was asking that a window be closed because of a draught. He needed a well-paid administrative post — secured with the help of his niece **Catherine Barton** and **Charles Montagu, Earl of Halifax** (Chancellor of the Exchequer, with whom Newton's relationship — via Catherine — was advantageous).

**The adversary: William Chaloner** — a flamboyant, classic super-villain counterpart:
- Born obscure in Birmingham; trained as a smith and nail-maker (poorly paid trades), but used metalworking skills for the more lucrative crime of **forging coins** and gilding cheap metal to pass as gold/silver.
- A serial fraudster: faked medical tinctures, posed as a soothsayer, predicted where stolen goods could be found (having stolen them himself), made and sold quack devices. Leibniz's biographer Guzmán Redivivus called him a "D-does &c in 'em" / "Tin Watches." "Highly sophisticated stuff."
- **Coin clipping & counterfeiting context:** England's currency was in dire straits. Old hammered silver coins had no milled edges, so "**clippers**" shaved slivers of silver off the edges — an estimated **10 percent of all coins** in circulation were counterfeit or clipped, made by people like Chaloner. Traders hoarded good English coins, melted them, and shipped silver abroad (Paris/Amsterdam) for profit because the coins were worth more as metal on the Continent than their face value at home — the currency was disappearing.

**Newton's Mint reforms (the science of money):**
- Newton drove the **Great Recoinage** — a complete replacement of Britain's currency. All old coins were withdrawn from circulation and replaced with new, more secure **milled-edge** coins (edges marked to make clipping detectable).
- He applied scientific method to production: analyzed and timed every step, measured machines and processes, optimized hammer frequency, press pressure, and worker output. He raised the Mint's output from about **15,000 pounds' worth of new coins per week** to **50,000**, with highs up to **100,000 pounds per week** — "no surprise that he was extremely successful in all this."

**Chaloner's provocation:** Chaloner didn't merely forge — he publicly attacked Newton, accusing the Royal Mint of corruption and "dodgy dealings," and proposed himself (in pamphlets like *"Proposals Humbly Offered, for Passing an Act to Prevent Clipping and Counterfeiting of Money"*) as the man who should run the Mint to stop counterfeiting — the very crime he committed. He petitioned Parliament for access to the Mint's secret machines and methods. This made Newton "a sworn enemy."

### CUT TO THE QUICK (pp. 35–37) — Newton hunts Chaloner down
- As Warden, Newton's duties included pursuing counterfeiters. He pursued Chaloner with "the same wholehearted persistence he applied to everything else."
- He built a personal **network of informers** across London, disguised himself, frequented taverns and jails in suitable clothing, and personally interrogated suspects, spies, and informers held at the Mint. The written records of these interrogations mostly disappeared (likely destroyed by Newton himself) — "he didn't want posterity to hear about the interrogation methods he used."
- Chaloner was arrested. The banks would explain how the forged money could have been used to prevent this freedom; the Royal Mint closed ranks. Chaloner appealed, manipulated, secured release once, then was rearrested.
- **1698:** Chaloner jailed again, this time for forging lottery tickets; conviction was inevitable. Newton methodically gathered evidence, prepared a scientific-style treatise (brief) proving each of Chaloner's offenses, and lined up witnesses, questioning them for up to **ten days in a row**.
- Chaloner, increasingly desperate, wrote begging letters to Newton: *"I am not guilty of any Crime,"* then *"I have been guilty of no Crime,"* escalating to *"if I die I am murdered"* — and finally, in his last letter (returned 14 March 1699): *"O God my God I shall be murdered unless you save me. O I hope God will move your heart with mercy and pitty to do this thing for me. I am Your near murdered humble Servant."*
- **Newton gave no response.** Chaloner was found guilty and **hanged on April 1, 1699.** Newton's relentless technique "had always worked so well."

### IN PRAISE OF MODERATE RUTHLESSNESS (pp. 37–40) — author's reflection
The chapter closes by translating Newton's character into advice for modern scientists:
- Newton was uncompromising, pigheaded, mad — "far beyond the clichéd image of the obdurate, mad professor." Yet the stereotype that *all* scientists are nerdish and detached (à la *The Big Bang Theory*) is false; it's fine to be a bit odd, but it is by no means a prerequisite for great science.
- **Why a degree of inconsiderateness can help:** Modern science is dominated by competition for scarce resources — grants, fellowships, project funding, research positions. Vacant positions are rare; you don't get many chances. Universities and research institutions offer a "protected environment" unlike many other professions, but to thrive you often can't simply follow strict guidelines and majority opinion — "a certain uncompromising approach to your colleagues is needed."
- **Solitary genius is largely over.** In Newton's day a lone wolf could push science forward by sheer correctness (ordering melons aside). Today's discoveries — the **Higgs boson** at **CERN**, **gravitational waves** detected by **LIGO**, the mapping of the **cosmic microwave background** by the **Planck satellite** — require huge international collaborations of hundreds or thousands of people. These "could never have been accomplished by uncooperative mavericks like Newton."
- **And yet:** a tiny bit of inconsiderateness — courage and self-confidence — does young scientists no harm. A team needs people willing to go against majority opinion; the majority isn't always right. If you never dare present your own opinion, you might miss a major breakthrough. Universities have become more like a "regimented system for education," rewarding quick degree completion over independent thought. PhDs too often merely accept assignments from professors. *"If you don't quickly learn to stand up for your own opinions, you'll never have the chance to tread a scientific path and come up with your own scientific results."*
- **Bottom line / role-model verdict:** You can and should be "a little inconsiderate of others" if you want to forge ahead — you needn't go fully over the top like Newton did. For all his genius, the *young* Newton is mainly a cautionary "troublemaker." But the *older* Newton — who took a well-paid official administration post (Master of the Mint) that printed his own money and left room for his real interests — is "definitely not a bad career move." Imitate the elder, pragmatic Newton, not the eye-stabbing youth.

## Key Definitions & Terms
- **Woolsthorpe** — Newton's home village in Lincolnshire; where he returned during plague years and began foundational work.
- **Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society** — founded 1665, the oldest continuously published scientific journal; in Newton's era it printed both genuine science and credulous curiosities.
- **Clipping** — shaving silver from the un-milled edges of hammered coins; rampant counterfeiting that, with melting/export, was draining England's currency (~10% of coins corrupted).
- **Milled edges** — the security feature (marked/reeded coin edges) Newton's recoinage introduced so clipping became detectable.
- **Warden / Master of the Royal Mint** — Newton's post from 1696; carried police powers to prosecute counterfeiters.
- **Great Recoinage** — Newton's total replacement of Britain's currency, scaled up via process optimization from ~15,000 to up to 100,000 pounds/week.
- **William Chaloner** — master counterfeiter and con-man who publicly attacked the Mint; Newton hunted him to the gallows (hanged April 1, 1699).
- **Humphry Newton** — Newton's Cambridge assistant (no relation) whose accounts document Newton's obsessive habits.

## Key Takeaways
1. Newton was simultaneously a foundational genius and a genuinely unpleasant person; the book treats the combination as the central, serious question rather than dismissing either half.
2. His defining trait was **ruthlessness without limits** — toward his own body (the needle-in-the-eye and sun-staring experiments), toward students (lecturing to empty halls), and toward enemies (driving Chaloner to execution and erasing the records).
3. 17th-century "natural science" was steeped in credulity (vomiting salamanders, talking dogs, melon-ordering); Newton's insistence on rigor made him both right and insufferable to his peers.
4. The same obsessive, optimizing mind that built physics also reformed the Royal Mint with quantitative process management.
5. **Lesson for modern scientists:** a *moderate* dose of Newton's stubborn self-confidence helps you resist groupthink and stand up for original results — but his lone-wolf extremity is obsolete in an age of CERN/LIGO/Planck-scale collaboration. Emulate the pragmatic elder administrator, not the self-destructive young fanatic.

# Chapter 2 — Principia First: Newton the Egoist (pp. 42–66)

*The chapter uses Newton's feud with Astronomer Royal John Flamsteed as a case study in his egoism, and closes by drawing a parallel to modern open-data disputes in science.*

## The Flamsteed–Newton relationship
- **John Flamsteed** wanted only to observe the heavens and produce the best, most comprehensive star catalogue ever made. He died before seeing his life's work published — and Newton was one of the people responsible for that.
- Flamsteed, October 1715, to a friend: Newton "very ungratefully set himself to hinder the work by several tricks and artifices... gave me all the trouble he could," adding he was treated "unworthily, nay, treacherously... by Sir I. Newton."
- The relationship started off relatively harmonious but Newton's ego made conflict "unavoidable" — he was too sure of himself to admit mistakes, and Flamsteed (who hadn't caused the dispute and initially wanted to help Newton) was the one who suffered.
- Newton's *Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) laid the foundation of modern science — a mathematical analysis of gravity and proof that the same physical laws govern Earth and the heavens (cannonballs follow the same rules as planets and comets). To demonstrate this, Newton needed concrete observational data — which is where Flamsteed came in.

## Flamsteed as Astronomer Royal
- March 1675: royal decree made Flamsteed "the King's Astronomical Observer" — the first **Astronomer Royal** (today an honorary title; then it carried defined duties).
- His job: observe and chart star positions accurately enough to help solve the **problem of determining longitude at sea** — important enough that the English king founded a dedicated observatory (Greenwich) for it.

## Looking for longitude (background science)
- **Latitude** (distance north/south of the equator) was already solvable in the 17th century: observe the sun's zenith position, especially at the **equinoxes** (the two days/year when day and night are equal length and the sun is exactly perpendicular to the equator). Works with stars at night too.
- **Longitude** (distance east/west from a reference line) was far harder, for two reasons: (1) there's no natural reference point — any prime meridian is arbitrary (today's runs through Greenwich, not coincidentally where Flamsteed worked); (2) it requires knowing the *time* precisely, and accurate clocks didn't yet exist.
- 17th-century timekeeping: sundials, hourglasses, water clocks, simple mechanical clockworks — accurate only to about an hour. Pendulum clocks (newly invented, second half of 1600s) were more accurate but too heavy/unwieldy for ships at sea.
- **Why time matters for longitude:** the earth rotates west-to-east, so "solar noon" (when the sun reaches its zenith) happens at different clock times in different places. If a ship's captain knows what time it is *right now* at Greenwich (where Flamsteed's clock shows the sun already at its peak) and compares it to his own local solar time, the difference tells him his longitude.
  - Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours → **15° of longitude per hour** of time difference.
  - Example given: if the sun is at its zenith in Greenwich exactly one hour before it peaks for a ship in the North Atlantic, the ship is exactly 15° west of Greenwich.
- Problem: there was no way for a captain to instantly know "what time is it right now in Greenwich" — no such clock existed. Flamsteed's proposed solution: build an astronomical "celestial clock" — a star catalogue precise enough to read time from the sky.
  - A **solar eclipse** could work as a universal timing event but is too rare to be practical (happens too infrequently to help navigate day-to-day).
  - Better: the **moon**, which moves visibly against the star background every night. If you know the moon's path and exact star positions, you can calculate Greenwich time from how far the moon is from a given star (the "**lunar-distances method**") — the moon as the clock's hand, the stars as its numbers. A captain with a thick reference book of moon/star positions could measure local time, compare to the looked-up Greenwich time, and calculate longitude.

## Newton ignores the celestial clock and common decency
- Flamsteed's job: measure star positions. Newton's parallel job: use his theory of gravity to calculate the moon's trajectory mathematically. In principle, a perfect partnership.
- 1694–1696: the two worked fairly harmoniously, but Newton failed to complete a satisfactory lunar theory, then got distracted by his new job at the **Royal Mint** and shelved it.
- 1703: Newton became **president of the Royal Society** and returned to lunar theory, planning a new edition of the *Principia* including a comprehensive mathematical study of the moon's motion — for which he needed Flamsteed's fresh observational data.
- April 1704: the two met at Greenwich. Flamsteed was in principle equally invested in the lunar-distances method (his observatory existed for exactly this purpose) but **disliked Newton's approach** — Flamsteed wanted to publish his own multivolume life's work (working title: *Historia Coelestis Britannica*) on his own terms: instruments/methods first, then a catalogue of thousands of star positions, then moon/planet/comet observations.
- **Newton didn't care about any of that** — he wanted only the moon/planet data, considered everything else superfluous, and expected Flamsteed to drop everything and subordinate his own work to Newton's needs.
- Newton's cajoling letter promised they'd both become famous if Flamsteed cooperated. Flamsteed wasn't convinced, calling Newton's manner "hasty, artificial, unkind, arrogant" — backed up by Newton's own words: "I consider this theory to be so complex and the theory of gravitation so necessary for its understanding that I am convinced that it can never be perfected by somebody who does not understand the theory of gravitation as well as I, or better than I."

## Newton piles on the pressure
- Newton believed he alone could solve lunar motion and that Flamsteed was obligated to simply hand over data. Flamsteed refused to be rushed — he wanted to vet and present his painstaking observations properly, especially after a previous bad experience: he'd once given Newton data on condition it not be passed to others, and Newton **passed it to a colleague anyway without crediting Flamsteed** as the source.
- Newton used his influence — gained via his niece **Catherine Barton**'s relationship with **Baron Halifax** (the queen's chancellor) — to get **Prince George** (Queen Anne's husband) to issue a royal edict ordering Flamsteed's data published immediately, with funding provided.
- Flamsteed couldn't defy a royal edict but slow-walked it: he hired two assistants using Newton's funding but had them work on his star catalogue (which Newton didn't want) instead of the moon/planet data Newton was demanding — so Newton refused to pay them.
- Author's view: the dispute was "childish" — if Newton had simply let Flamsteed work at his own pace he'd have gotten the data eventually. But Newton, unlike Flamsteed, wasn't actually trying to solve the navigation problem — he just wanted to finish an improved *Principia*.

## Newton forces publication; Flamsteed's revenge
- Newton (via the royal edict, with help from natural scientist/royal doctor **John Arbuthnot**) had Flamsteed's **sealed envelope** of observations opened and started publishing the incomplete data without consulting him — leaving Flamsteed "shocked" to see decades of work mishandled.
- Flamsteed to Arbuthnot, refusing to be placated: "I have now spent 35 years in composeing & Work of my Catalogue... I have endured long and painfull distempers by my night watches & Day Labours, I have spent a large sum of Money above my appointment, out of my own Estate to complete my Catalogue."
- Pay disparity: Flamsteed earned only **£100/year** as Astronomer Royal (not enough to live on — he needed a second job as a parish priest) while Newton, as a Royal Mint official, made **at least £1,500/year**. Newton paid the printer over £1/page while Flamsteed got nothing.
- 1708: Prince George died; Flamsteed treated the royal edict as defunct and stopped cooperating. Newton retaliated by getting Flamsteed excluded from the Royal Society (ostensibly for an unpaid membership fee).
- 1710: Newton obtained a fresh edict from Queen Anne herself and got himself made a kind of supervisor of Greenwich Observatory — **becoming Flamsteed's boss** outright, and dropped all pretense of civility, effectively accusing Flamsteed of treason for withholding data and threatening that delay "will be taken for an indirect refusal to comply with her Majesty's order."
- **1712:** the catalogue — titled *Historia Coelestis* — was published under Newton's control without Flamsteed's involvement, using his "stolen" data; Newton's foreword publicly accused Flamsteed of self-interest and poor-quality data.
- Newton then used the data for a new *Principia* edition with an updated (but still inaccurate) lunar theory — and carefully erased every acknowledgment of Flamsteed's contribution from the first edition.
- **1714:** Queen Anne died, Newton lost his court influence, and Flamsteed had the unsold copies of Newton's catalogue pulled from the market and **publicly burned them in the observatory garden** — then resumed publishing his own version.
- Flamsteed died **five years later** (without finishing); his widow and two assistants completed it at their own expense. **1725:** *Historia Coelestis Britannica* was finally published as Flamsteed always intended — the best star catalogue of its time, securing his legacy.
- **1884:** the Greenwich meridian was internationally adopted as the **prime meridian** for time and location, largely because so many astronomers had relied on Flamsteed's catalogue. The original observatory building is now Flamsteed House, a museum honoring him.

## The fine line between persistence and hubris
- The dispute showed Newton at his "most unpleasant" — vengeful, small-minded, vindictive, selfish, and convinced any problem was someone else's fault.
- Newton never considered that his own (mathematically immature) methods, not Flamsteed's data, were the real reason his lunar theory failed — a three-body problem (sun–earth–moon gravitational interaction) that cannot be solved exactly and that Newton lacked the tools to approximate. It was only solved practically by later astronomers: **Tobias Mayer** (German) and **Nicolas-Louis de Lacaille** (French) by 1753, and ultimately superseded for navigation by **John Harrison's** marine chronometers (though the lunar-distances method stayed in use as a cheaper backup into the second half of the 19th century).
- Author's balanced take: modern science *requires* peer scrutiny and criticism — researchers who refuse outside evaluation of their methods/results aren't really part of the modern scientific process, and ideally self-criticism should come before others'. In that light Newton was wrong to simply blame Flamsteed instead of questioning his own approach.
- But Newton's underlying demand — that publicly funded research data be made public — has more merit today, when basic research is largely funded by taxpayers.

## Modern parallel: the Rosetta mission data dispute
- **2014 controversy:** the European Space Agency's **Rosetta** mission landed on comet **67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko** (taxpayer-funded via ESA member states). The public, having followed the historic landing closely, expected fast access to photos/data, but ESA scientists released images and data only sparingly, wanting first right to work with their own results.
- Same structural conflict as Newton vs. Flamsteed: scientists who'd spent decades building the probe wanted first use of their findings (publications are "the currency of modern research" and career-critical), versus the argument that publicly funded findings belong to everyone and wider access accelerates discovery.
- **Compromise reached:** ESA's own scientists got temporary priority access (standard international practice) while some current images were still released promptly for public benefit.
- **Takeaway:** Newton's personal egoism shouldn't be a model for scientists today, but his obstinate insistence on open data access does set a reasonable precedent — ironically, since Newton himself would have been "extremely surprised" to be cast as an advocate for open data.

# Chapter 3 — False Modesty and Easily Offended (pp. 68–90)

*Uses Newton's early career — his secretive habits, the invention of the reflecting telescope, and his color/optics feud with Robert Hooke — to explore his hypersensitivity to criticism, then pivots to explain and defend the modern peer-review system.*

## Newton's fear of criticism, rooted in astrology
- Newton: "Truth is the offspring of silence and meditation" — sounds Zen, but he was anything but calm when criticized: hypersensitive, quick to fight back, with "countless extensive and vigorous disputes" across his career.
- Early in his career he tried to dodge criticism by publishing **anonymously** — a habit whose consequences shaped decades of his life. Many of his feuds might never have happened without this fear of others' criticism.
- Trigger for his mathematical career: in 1664, fresh out of Cambridge undergraduate studies, Newton couldn't understand a diagram in a book on **astrology**. University courses of the time focused on theology and the classics, not math, so Newton **taught himself mathematics** from scratch — and once he started something he never stopped until he'd thought through every aspect, graduating from self-study into research at the frontier of existing mathematical knowledge.

## In search of infinity
- Newton's notebooks show calculations of **infinite series** (sums of infinitely many numbers that nonetheless converge to a finite value if terms shrink fast enough) — in one case worked out to the 55th decimal place. He wrote in 1666: "I am ashamed to tell you to how many figures I carried these computations, having no other business at the time... I had too much pleasure in these discoveries."
- Infinity was already a math research topic but still considered mysterious; **René Descartes** (whose work Newton studied closely) wrote in *Principles of Philosophy* that questions like "is half of an infinite line also infinite" weren't worth engaging with. Newton disagreed and engaged deeply — his work on infinity led to a wholly new mathematical discipline (foreshadowing calculus, covered in chapter 7).
- He worked alone, in secret. Only **Isaac Barrow** (Cambridge's mathematics chair since 1664) knew what Newton was doing and recognized its importance; Barrow eventually convinced him to share some findings.
- 1668: Barrow showed Newton a book by German mathematician **Nicholas Mercator** on infinite series — Newton was shocked to see his own unpublished ideas already in print, though his own work was far more comprehensive. At Barrow's insistence, Newton wrote up a short account.
- Barrow sent it to mathematician **John Collins**, a hub for distributing new mathematical work across Europe (the era's equivalent of an internet forum). Newton only agreed to this on condition Collins **not reveal his name** — "I see not what there is desirable in public esteem were I able to acquire and maintain it." He was finally persuaded to go public only after Collins's reply contained nothing but enthusiastic praise — the first time Newton's work became known outside Cambridge, beginning an extensive but increasingly frustrating correspondence.

## Newton constructs a telescope
- Newton played hard to get with Collins: dangling results he'd never send, refusing the "drudgery" of writing up a requested table, and repeatedly insisting his name be left off any publication of his answers, even an annotation he made to a Dutch mathematician's book ("extended by another author," not "by Isaac Newton").
- Author's aside: Newton could have been world-famous immediately on the strength of his math alone, but his complete indifference to public opinion likely held back the development of the natural sciences and spared him nothing — his "biggest and longest" career dispute was still coming (chapter 7, re: calculus priority with Leibniz).
- Around the same time, Newton invented a wholly practical counterpart to his theoretical work: a working **reflecting telescope**. Telescopes had existed ~60 years (Galileo used them; credit for the original invention, c. 1608, is disputed between Dutch opticians **Hans Lipperhey**, **Zacharias Janssen**, or astronomer **Adriaan Metius**).
- **How a (refracting) telescope works:** a converging (convex) lens focuses parallel light rays to a point; a diverging (concave) lens spreads them back into parallel rays so the eye can focus the image. The converging lens — much bigger than the eye's pupil — gathers far more light, letting faint objects be seen. Bigger lens = more light = need a longer tube to focus it, hence long, unwieldy lens telescopes.
- Existing lens telescopes were flawed: images were fuzzy and colors appeared wrong. Newton, uniquely, understood why, thanks to his optics research (including dangerous self-experiments — sticking a needle into his own eye/eye socket to study perception).
- **1665:** Cambridge closed due to plague; Newton retreated to his birthplace, **Woolsthorpe**, where in quiet isolation he ran what's regarded as one of history's most important experiments: a hole in a darkened room's wall let a narrow beam of sunlight through a **prism**, splitting it into colors.
- It was already known prisms produced colors from light, and assumed the prism itself generated the colors. Newton tested this: he isolated a single color of light from the first rainbow (via a small hole in a screen) and passed just that ray through a **second prism**. No new rainbow appeared — blue stayed blue, red stayed red.
- **Conclusion: the prism doesn't create colors, it separates them.** White light is a mixture of all colors. Newton proved this further by recombining the full spectrum through a second prism back into white light. He also found light **changes direction (refracts) by different amounts per color** when passing through glass — blue deviates more than red.
- This explained **chromatic aberration**: in a lens telescope, each color refracts a different amount, so no single sharp image forms — instead several overlapping, slightly-offset colored images. The fix: use **mirrors** instead of lenses, since reflection (unlike refraction) doesn't separate colors — already known in principle, but nobody had built a working mirror telescope until Newton.
- **1668**, after Cambridge reopened: Newton built his reflecting telescope. He made every part himself, including necessary tools — "If I had waited for other people to make my tools and design things for me, I had never made anything," he told his niece's husband.
- Newton's telescope was only ~30 cm long yet achieved roughly **35x magnification**, almost 3x better than the best lens telescopes of the day (which needed much longer tubes for similar power). He cast the main mirror himself from **speculum metal** (copper/tin alloy), ~33 cm across, paired with a smaller secondary mirror that diverted the reflected image out through a side opening so the observer's head didn't block incoming light.

## Raise the curtain for Newton's favorite enemy
- Collins and Barrow again heard of the invention first; Barrow brought the telescope to the **Royal Society** in London in 1671, impressing members. Secretary **Henry Oldenburg** asked Newton to publish a report and apply for a patent (to stop others claiming the design), and invited him to join the Society.
- True to form, Newton reacted coyly — "I was surprised to see so much care taken about securing an invention to me, of which I have hitherto had so little value" — but eventually sent details for publication (again pushing for anonymity) and accepted membership, flattered by the praise. He referred to sharing his future "poor and solitary endeavours" — likely false modesty.
- In a follow-up letter he reversed course, calling his underlying research "in my judgment the oddest if not the most considerable detection which hath hitherto beene made in the operations of Nature." So much for modesty.

## The feud with Robert Hooke begins
- **February 1672:** Newton sent Oldenburg his article "New Theory about Light and Colours." Read to the Royal Society two days later, it triggered exactly what Newton always feared: feedback. A committee was formed to evaluate it, with **Robert Hooke** writing the final report — making himself Newton's lifelong enemy in the process.
- Hooke (7 years Newton's senior) was already an established scientist: Royal Society member since 1663, its first curator of experiments, and known for his microscope work. His illustrated book ***Micrographia*** (1665) — drawings of enlarged insects, needlepoints, razor blades, mildew — was a scientific bestseller (1,200 copies sold out within days).
- *Micrographia* had also proposed a color theory: light arises from vibration/movement, with only **two basic colors (red and blue)** combining in the eye to produce others ("many sorts of greens," etc.) — a theory Hooke admitted he hadn't fully explained ("It would take a little too long to explain... how I found out the characteristics of light") while still claiming to have solved color.
- Newton, who'd literally stuck needles in his own eyes to study light, was predictably irritated by someone claiming complete knowledge without showing the work — especially since his own Woolsthorpe experiments had convinced him Hooke's theory was wrong.
- Hooke counter-claimed he'd already done Newton's described experiments earlier, and that Newton had misinterpreted his own results. Newton was outraged: he considered his findings not a hypothesis but mathematical certainty — "without any suspicion of doubt" — and called Hooke's theory "not only insufficient, but in some respects unintelligible."
- Author's assessment: Newton's optics conclusions were (largely) correct, but he'd had years to develop and refine them in private while the rest of the world was encountering the ideas for the first time — he should have expected an adjustment period and pushback, but wasn't prepared for it. His first real public exposure "must have seemed... quite a disaster."

## Just like in kindergarten
- Over the following years: **10 critical articles** in the *Philosophical Transactions* and **11 angry replies** from Newton. He especially hated his findings being called "hypotheses" rather than mathematical fact, particularly from Hooke.
- Newton refused to accept even valid criticism — e.g., from Dutch scholar **Christiaan Huygens**, who correctly noted white light can also be produced by mixing just two complementary colors (like blue and yellow), not only the full spectrum. Newton reacted with rage regardless and threatened (but didn't follow through) to quit the Royal Society "to avoid such things in future."
- The Hooke feud escalated to physics fundamentals: Hooke held light was a **wave**; Newton held it was a stream of **particles**. Hooke claimed an experiment proving light's wave nature; Newton said the experiment was older and not Hooke's to claim; Hooke accused Newton of lifting ideas from *Micrographia* uncredited. Author: "the two of them behaved as though they were in kindergarten."
- (The wave-vs-particle question was only definitively resolved centuries later, with the advent of quantum mechanics.)
- After this clash, Newton largely stopped publishing for a long stretch — including keeping his major mathematical writings and his work on gravity to himself, unknown to peers. His bruising first encounter with public criticism seems to have taught him to simply go silent rather than face public judgment again — ingenious, revolutionary work that almost nobody got to learn from because he was, despite the ego, "a shrinking violet."

## Good criticism is half the battle (modern parallel: peer review)
- Newton's instinct to refuse all critique would be "disastrous" for science today — constant review is the foundation of modern research, however unpleasant. What the Royal Society did to Newton's optics paper is now standardized as **peer review**.
- Process: a paper submitted to a journal first passes an editor's formal check (fit, writing quality, structure, sourcing), then goes to expert reviewers in the same field, who scrutinize methods/results, try to reproduce findings, flag errors or gaps, and recommend accept/reject; authors must address feedback before publication.
- Goal: minimize **false results**, not necessarily catch deliberate fraud — mainly to catch the honest mistakes everyone makes, since true scientific objectivity is hard for inherently subjective humans (we tend to overlook errors that conveniently support our own hypothesis).
- **Author's personal example:** while studying possible planets around the star **Beta Pictoris** using simulations, the author was delighted to "explain" observed features with a single hypothetical planet — until a colleague's informal review flagged "something isn't right... too good to be true." The author's first reaction mirrored Newton's: annoyance. But re-checking found a small bug in the code that had been producing the wanted result by coincidence — exactly the kind of error peer review exists to catch.
- **Einstein example:** even great scientists struggle with criticism. In 1936, Einstein and Nathan Rosen submitted a paper to *Physical Review* concluding that gravitational waves — which Einstein himself had predicted 20 years earlier — didn't actually exist. An anonymous reviewer found a mathematical mistake in the paper. Einstein, furious that the editor had shown the unpublished manuscript to anyone without his authorization, withdrew it and vowed never to publish in that journal again (a vow he kept for life). The corrected paper, published a year later elsewhere, reversed course and concluded gravitational waves *could* exist — showing Einstein ultimately took the criticism on board despite his initial defiance.
- **Takeaway:** working scientists must accept ongoing peer evaluation; being a genius doesn't exempt you from criticism, as Newton himself was about to be reminded again in his next round with Hooke (leading into Chapter 4).

# Chapter 4 — Gravity without the Apple: Newton's Pugnacious Side (pp. 92–112)

*Debunks/contextualizes the apple myth, walks through how Newton actually arrived at universal gravitation, covers "Round 2" of the Hooke feud (the falling-ball/orbit dispute that led to the* Principia*), and closes with a defense of scientific dispute as necessary but not unlimited license for bad behavior.*

## The apple myth
- Famous origin story: an apple fell on Newton's head, inspiring the theory of gravity — ranked with other dubious science legends (Galileo dropping objects from the Leaning Tower of Pisa, Archimedes running naked shouting "Eureka!" after his bathtub insight). Modern history doubts the Pisa and bathtub stories; the apple story is "different too."
- What's well documented: developing the theory of gravity involved "no end of quarreling" with Newton's archenemy **Robert Hooke**.

## Uncertain knowledge about the universe
- In Newton's day, "gravity" was as ill-defined as "light" had been (chapter 3). Everyone knew things fell, but not why, what made the moon orbit the earth, or even what "motion" fundamentally was.
- Contemporary theories of gravity were varied and often bizarre: Newton's own notebook described it as something material — a "gravitational liquid" contained in all matter, causing "massiness." Others saw it as a kind of radiation, or as a *place* — a special central location all objects were drawn toward, which (since things fall down) had to be the center of the earth, doubling as the center of the universe. This created absurdities: why would a falling stone be drawn to the same point as a falling object on the literal opposite side of the planet? And if gravity were a liquid, it should logically pool in "giant hollow spaces in the earth's interior" — an idea Newton actually entertained in his notes.
- The vocabulary itself was missing: no settled definitions for "motion," "space," "gravity," "force"; no standardized units or measuring instruments.
- Newton read predecessors' experimental data closely, e.g. transcribing Galileo's reported fall-rate figures in a hodgepodge of period units (Florentine pounds, London avoirdupois, braces, Ells) — the only unit from that note still used today is the **second**.

## And so it fell...
- Per **William Stukeley**'s 1752 biography (a friend of Newton's), the apple was real but didn't hit Newton on the head — it just fell to the ground near him, same as any apple, while he happened to be sitting nearby with nothing better to do (Cambridge was shut for the plague).
- This happened during Newton's 1665–1666 stay at **Woolsthorpe** — the same plague-exile period that produced his prism experiments (ch. 1, ch. 3) and his early mathematics (ch. 3, ch. 7). Virtually all his most revolutionary ideas date to this one stretch.

## The apple-to-moon reasoning
- Watching the apple fall, Newton wondered whether the same influence that pulls an apple to earth also keeps the **moon** in orbit. Apple and moon both move toward the earth, just differently — the apple falls straight down, the moon "hangs" and circles.
- Key insight via visual proportion: held at the same apparent (angular) size, a nearby small apple looks identical to the much larger, much more distant moon — because apparent size shrinks with distance. Newton worked out that this relationship isn't linear: if the moon were twice as close, its apparent surface area would be **4x** bigger; four times closer, **16x** bigger. Surface-area appearance scales with the **square** of the change in distance — what's mathematically called an **inverse-square relationship**.
- Newton hypothesized gravity might follow the same inverse-square law, and using the (limited, somewhat inaccurate) figures then available for the moon's size/distance, did a rough calculation confirming that a force following an inverse-square law was indeed consistent with both the apple's and the moon's behavior.
- Reality check on the "eureka moment" myth: this Woolsthorpe insight was only the *starting point*, not a finished theory — much harder work followed. Newton returned to Cambridge, threw himself into math, built his telescope, published on light/color, fought with the Royal Society (chs. 1, 3), then moved to entirely different subjects (ch. 6). He didn't return to gravity until the 1680s — prompted by a comet.

## The wrath of the comet
- 17th-century comets were a total mystery: unknown composition, sudden unpredictable appearances/disappearances (unlike the regular, predictable stars and planets), broadly seen as **omens of disaster** — a view traceable back to Pliny the Elder's *Natural History* (77 CE) and still essentially unchanged in 1680.
- When a comet appeared at the end of **1680**, panic followed: a Nuremberg pamphlet called it a "sword of vengeance and rod of wrath of Almighty God," a warning meant to bring "dread and transformation in hardened sinful souls." Hundreds of similar alarmist pamphlets and leaflets circulated.
- Newton and other Royal Society scientists (Edmond Halley, then only 21; Robert Hooke; Astronomer Royal John Flamsteed) tracked the comet closely. Flamsteed missed its initial appearance (visible only briefly before dawn) but predicted, correctly, that the same comet would reappear — it did, in mid-December — suggesting comets might behave more predictably/regularly than assumed.
- Flamsteed wrote to Newton (via a Cambridge friend; their later bitter feud, ch. 2, was still years off) proposing a vague theory: the sun attracts comets (maybe magnetically), pulling them in on a straight path until they're repelled again at close range — like magnetism's attract/repel behavior.
- Newton rebutted bluntly: the sun is hot, and magnetism is destroyed by heat, so a magnetic mechanism made no sense. He also wasn't sure the "comet" wasn't actually two separate objects, since his own observations showed inconsistent speed. But he proposed an alternative: even without a straight line in/out, an exclusively **attracting force from the sun** could make the comet move on a curved path *around* the sun — a force-based, not magnetism-based, explanation. This effort to mathematically describe interdependent forces from the sun, earth, moon, etc. would prove central to his later work.

## Hooke v. Newton: Round 2
- The comet prompted Newton's first concrete public statement on the cause of celestial motion — and triggered another Hooke clash. Hooke had separately been thinking about planetary/cometary motion and (unaware Newton had had the same idea back in 1666, since Newton never told anyone) asked Newton's opinion in 1679, partly to patch up their optics feud. Hooke proposed an attractive force whose strength varied with distance; Newton played dumb about having thought of this before, but did engage on a related thought experiment.
- **The falling-ball/tower problem:** drop a ball from a tall tower — where does it land? Since the earth rotates, and a point higher up moves faster (greater tangential velocity) than the ground below, the ball should retain some of that extra eastward speed and land slightly **east** of the tower's base, not exactly at its foot and not west of it.
- Newton went further, illustrating in a diagram that if a ball could fall **unobstructed straight through the earth** toward the center, the planet's rotation would mean it actually traces a **spiral** path toward the center, not a literal straight line.
- Hooke called this "nonsense" — correctly, per the author — arguing the ball would instead behave like an orbiting body moving along an **elliptical** path around the earth's middle. Newton, predictably enraged at being corrected by Hooke of all people, was further angered when Hooke (breaking an agreement to keep the exchange private) read Newton's letters aloud publicly to Royal Society members.
- Newton hit back with a corrected diagram showing the (also somewhat-off, mathematically vague) elliptical path more precisely. The two argued further over how a body's path relates to another body's force of attraction. Hooke's final position: the force's strength is **inversely proportional to the square of the distance** (inverse-square law), and he challenged Newton to mathematically derive what orbital path such a force would produce. Newton, despite his mathematical skill, simply went silent rather than answer.

## The silence is broken
- **1684:** Edmond Halley, Hooke, and architect/scientist **Christopher Wren** met at a coffee house and discussed the same question — all three suspected an inverse-square law governed celestial motion but lacked rigorous proof. Hooke claimed he'd already solved it mathematically but was keeping it secret until it would be properly appreciated (i.e., he was likely bluffing).
- Unconvinced, Halley visited Newton at Cambridge in August 1684 and asked directly: what path results from an inverse-square attractive force? **Newton answered immediately: an ellipse** — he said he'd worked this out mathematically long ago but had lost the calculations, and promised to redo and send them to Halley.
- Remarkably, Newton followed through — first sending Halley a short manuscript, "On the Motion of Bodies in an Orbit," then kept expanding it: gathering comet/planet/moon data (including requesting observations from Flamsteed, years before their dispute), inventing precise new definitions for "space" and "time," and applying his own (still largely unknown) mathematics to explain not just planetary motion but many other terrestrial and celestial phenomena. This became an entirely new "natural philosophy" and the mathematical foundation of physics.
- The resulting work: ***Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica*** ("Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy"). An early partial draft impressed the Royal Society enough that they committed to print it, with Halley supervising.
- **Publication nearly derailed by fish:** the Society had just sunk money into printing naturalist Francis Willughby's heavily illustrated, posthumously published *History of Fish* (1686), which sold poorly and left the Society reluctant to fund another expensive book. Newton's *Principia* was almost shelved as a result — saved only because **Halley personally paid most of the printing costs out of pocket**. The Society's odd repayment to Halley: instead of a previously agreed £50 fee for other work done for them, they gave him 50 copies of the unsold *History of Fish*.

## A small theory for a big universe
- Shortly before final publication, the Hooke dispute reignited: in April 1686, Halley wrote Newton to relay that Hooke felt the inverse-square idea originated with him and should be credited in the *Principia*.
- Newton exploded in reply to Halley, dismissing mathematicians who "settle and do all the business" as mere "dry calculators & drudges" against people (Hooke) who "pretend & grasp at all things" and "carry away all the invention." He directly told Halley "Mr. Hook has erred in the invention he pretends to & his error is the cause of all the stir he makes." Newton conceded only that Hooke had mentioned the idea to him, unsolicited, and that he owed Hooke no credit since he hadn't asked for the information; if anything, Hooke's flawed early description had spurred Newton's own deeper work. He called Hooke "a man of a strange unsociable temper" and began stripping every mention of Hooke from his manuscript.
- Author's balanced judgment: Newton reacted "far too aggressively and far too quickly," as usual — but wasn't entirely wrong. Other scientists of the era had floated similar inverse-square *guesses*, but none had built the rigorous mathematical framework and unified worldview from which the law necessarily follows. **Only Newton did that** — which is the real significance of his work, not merely guessing the exponent.

## What's actually revolutionary about Newton's gravity
- Not "inventing" or "discovering" gravity per se, but proving gravity is a **single universal force** governing both an apple falling and a planet orbiting — formalized as a mathematical formula applicable to the whole universe (detailed in chapter 5). This itself forms the third volume of the *Principia*, titled *De mundi systemate* ("Of the System of the World").
- Newton showed natural laws are not local curiosities but universal and unifying — establishing the basis for modern science's drive to unify disparate phenomena under common laws (the same impulse driving modern physicists toward a "theory of everything" or unifying quantum mechanics with general relativity).
- Newton's gravity is technically "wrong": in 1915 **Einstein's general relativity** superseded it with a more accurate model. But Newton's equations remain enormously useful — accurate enough to navigate spacecraft precisely across the solar system — and are simpler/easier to calculate than Einstein's, which is why they're still used today within their known validity limits.

## Go in search of dispute! (closing argument)
- Could Newton have avoided so much quarreling? The author argues disputes in science are **unavoidable and necessary**: scientists are ordinary humans with the full range of human flaws, and confrontation is built into how science functions — reconciling the drive to discover new things with the need to rigorously test and sometimes reject new claims.
- A healthy tension: science must stay open to radically new ideas, yet must also be conservative about overturning well-tested theories on a whim. New ideas, by definition, are often-unproven speculation; peers exist to soberly assess whether a claim is genuinely revolutionary or just wishful thinking — disputes are a natural, even healthy, byproduct.
- Crucially: **science isn't democratic** — truth isn't decided by majority vote or split down the middle, and a lone researcher can be right even against unanimous peer opposition. So one shouldn't be cowed by criticism alone, nor assume being criticized automatically means being wrong; there's always a need to take legitimate critique seriously rather than escalating every disagreement into a fight.
- A degree of **pugnacity is a legitimate, even necessary, trait** for a successful scientist — you need to defend your ideas and be willing to challenge others', not just defer to authority. But this shouldn't be taken to Newton's extreme.
- One thing Newton should **not** be emulated on: refusing to acknowledge others' legitimate contributions, as he did to Hooke (and, per chapters 2 and 7, to Flamsteed and Leibniz). That's not confrontation or criticism — it's "unnecessarily impolite" at best and, at worst, **plagiarism** (presenting others' ideas/findings as your own) — a serious form of malpractice today regardless of how brilliant the plagiarist is.

# Chapters 5–6: On the Shoulders of… / In Search of the Philosopher's Stone

## Chapter 5: On the Shoulders of…

### Standing on Prior Giants
Newton acknowledged debts to predecessors: Kepler's planetary laws, Descartes' vortex theory (which he demolished), Galileo's motion work, and Huygens. The *Principia* (1687) synthesized all of these into a unified mathematical framework.

### The Invention of a New Language
Newton needed new mathematics to describe motion — he developed calculus (fluxions), though he kept it private. The *Principia* itself was written in classical geometry, deliberately difficult, to avoid being pestered by amateurs.

### Newton's Laws of Motion
1. **First Law (Inertia):** Every object moves in a straight line at constant speed unless acted on by a force.
2. **Second Law:** The change of motion is proportional to the force applied; F = ma.
3. **Third Law:** For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.

### A Universal Force
Newton showed the same force that makes an apple fall also keeps the Moon in orbit. He calculated the Moon should fall ~15 feet each minute if gravity follows an inverse-square law — and it does. He called this force "gravity."

### The Wild Nature of the Tides
Newton provided the first mathematical explanation of tides as caused by the differential gravitational pull of the Moon (and Sun) across the Earth. The tides are strongest when Sun and Moon align. Ancient thinkers including Galileo had struggled with this; Newton solved it with the *Principia*.

### The Man Who Didn't Want to Be Understood
Newton deliberately wrote the *Principia* in dense geometrical form. He wanted to make the work "Better Transparent Than Sorry" — avoiding public criticism by making it hard for all but the best mathematicians to follow. He was right about public reception being hostile to new ideas.

### Key Takeaway
Chapters 5–6 (first half) show Newton transforming natural philosophy from qualitative descriptions into a quantitative, predictive science — while simultaneously alienating almost everyone who tried to read his work.

## Chapter 6: In Search of the Philosopher's Stone

### Newton's Secret Life
Newton spent more time on theology and alchemy than on physics. He owned ~650,000 words on alchemy — approximately half of all surviving alchemical manuscript material from the era.

### The Philosopher's Stone
Newton believed in transmutation of base metals into gold. The alchemical community used coded language ("the Stone," "male/female" elements, "marriage" reactions). Newton collaborated in correspondence with figures like Robert Boyle and John Locke in pursuit of the Stone.

### A Prophet of Doom and an Alchemist
Newton was deeply religious — convinced the Bible contained coded prophecy. He spent years on *The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms* and *Observations Upon the Prophecies of Daniel*, predicting the end times. He was essentially a biblical prophet who happened to also invent calculus.

### Natural Sciences Without God
Newton believed God was not just a first cause but an active manager of the universe — keeping the planets in their orbits, correcting deviations. He saw gravity's action at a distance as evidence of God's continuous presence. He considered "atheism" an impossibility given the order of the cosmos.

### Seventy Years After Newton's Death
Laplace famously answered Napoleon's question about God in his celestial mechanics: "I had no need of that hypothesis." Newton's physics survived; his theology did not shape modern science — but it shaped Newton himself profoundly.

### Key Takeaways
- Newton was simultaneously the most rational and most mystical major figure in scientific history
- His alchemical and theological writing vastly outnumbers his physics writing
- The *Principia* represented one narrow slice of his intellectual life, not its center
- He died in 1727 still working on a manuscript, searching for hidden meanings in ancient texts

# Chapter 7: Beyond Death — Newton and His Imitators

## Newton Ends, Leibniz Begins
Newton spent his final years as Warden/Master of the Royal Mint and President of the Royal Society — essentially retired from science. His great antagonist was Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz.

## How Do You Describe Motion?
The ancient problem: Zeno's paradoxes (Achilles and the tortoise) exposed that describing continuous motion mathematically was unresolved for 2,000 years. The core issue is motion at an instant — what does "speed right now" mean when speed requires two points in time?

## Newton's Differential and Integral Calculus
Newton developed calculus between 1665–1666 in Woolsthorpe during the plague years. He called it the "method of fluxions." The key insight: treat infinitely small intervals (infinitesimals) as if they exist, calculate with them, then let them shrink to zero. This let you calculate the gradient (slope) of a curve at any point — the derivative — and the area under a curve — the integral.

Newton kept it private and didn't publish for decades. This would cost him.

## Leibniz — The Plagiarist?
Leibniz independently developed calculus in the 1670s–80s and published first (1684). He had visited London in 1676 and seen some of Newton's unpublished work. Newton accused him of plagiarism. Leibniz denied it.

## Newton Brings Out the Heavy Artillery
Newton weaponized the Royal Society (which he ran) to adjudicate the dispute. In 1712 the Society published a report declaring Leibniz a plagiarist — but Newton had secretly written the report himself. The committee was a sham.

## The Crux of Having to Be First
Science runs on priority. Newton and Leibniz both independently invented calculus — but in a world with no preprint servers, no journals, no internet, keeping your work private for decades was a catastrophic strategy. The dispute poisoned relations between British and Continental mathematics for a century: British mathematicians stuck with Newton's clunky notation; Leibniz's cleaner notation (dy/dx, ∫) is what the whole world uses today.

## The Aftermath
- Leibniz died in 1716 largely unrecognized, while Newton was celebrated.
- Over centuries historians concluded both invented calculus independently.
- Both were geniuses; both were assholes about credit.
- The lesson for modern science: plagiarism disputes are constant, the pressure to publish fast is immense, and the politics of scientific institutions corrupt even the greatest minds.

## Key Takeaway
Chapter 7 is about what happens *after* genius — the institutional politics, the ego battles, the priority wars. Newton invented calculus but lost the notation war. Leibniz published first but lost the reputation war. Modern science inherited both their methods and their bad habits.

# Conclusion: Newton's Legacy

## The Slight Exaggeration
Newton almost single-handedly laid the foundations of modern physics — but only almost. His idea of gravity as a force acting across empty space was incomprehensible to contemporaries (seeming like magic); yet it proved the seed of all modern field theory.

## Forces Become Fields
Newton's gravity was the first "action at a distance" force. Modern physics resolved this by showing forces propagate via fields — electromagnetic fields, quantum fields, the Standard Model. Photons arise from agitation of electromagnetic fields. Newton's uncomfortable mystery became the framework for all of modern physics.

## Unification
Newton's universal gravitation was the first unification in physics: one force explains both falling apples and orbiting planets. Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism. Einstein spent his career trying to unify electromagnetism and gravity. The "theory of everything" remains unfinished — but every step follows Newton's program.

## Newton the Person
- Eccentric, egoist, troublemaker, mystery-monger
- Tolerated no criticism; was uncompromising, vengeful, conniving
- Also the greatest scientific genius who ever lived
- No other scientist has had such wide-ranging, enduring influence on the entire world

## Final Line
*"Sometimes, it would seem, if you want to change the world, you have to be both a genius and an asshole."*
