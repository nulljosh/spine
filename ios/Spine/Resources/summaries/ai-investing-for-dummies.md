### Distinguishing the Two Types of AI
The author simplifies the broad AI landscape into **two types**: **General (horizontal)** AI and **Specific (vertical)** AI.

**General AI ("horizontal")**
- Spans the vast digital landscape to produce responses to general inquiries or requests.
- Good for starters and general knowledge.
- Example: **ChatGPT** — despite its impressive capabilities, the author wouldn't use it for highly specialized, high-stakes matters (e.g., a web page is fine, but not brain surgery or cancer diagnosis — he'd consult specialized human experts for those).
- Another general-purpose category: **AI-enhanced search engines** (18+ exist at the time of writing; listed in Appendix B).

**Specific AI ("vertical")**
- Concentrates on a given topic or specialty.
- Example: **robo-advisors** (covered in Chapter 7) — technological power applied to a narrow domain.
- Some AI tools specialize in finance, others in healthcare, and so on.
- **Analogy used:** Think of general AI like a general practitioner (GP) doctor — good for an initial visit — while specific AI is like a cardiologist, who you'd be referred to for specialized issues. The GP (general AI) helps you figure out what you need, then directs you to the specialist (specific AI).
- Chapter 3 covers different categories/directories of AI tools to help match the right tool to the right need.

### Investing in Stocks with AI's Help
- The primary reason readers picked up this book: the title combines "AI" and "investing," and the core of that combination is covered in **Part 2** of the book.
- Important distinction: **investing vs. speculating**.
  - *Investing* = putting money into assets/vehicles that take time to grow in value (e.g., large-cap stocks) — "educated gambling," hopefully with greater certainty over time.
  - *Speculating* = chasing quick, dramatic gains (e.g., small-cap or "ka-ching" tech stocks) — closer to true gambling; greater risk, greater (and faster) potential reward or loss.
  - Readers should know the difference between the two before deciding where AI fits into their strategy.
- For **large-cap stocks** (stable, established companies), see **Chapter 6** for fundamentals-based, longer-term analysis ("fundamentals" = the heart of true investing).
- For **small-cap/speculative stocks**, see **Chapter 8**.
- For a **safer approach to AI-related investing**, consider **ETFs (exchange-traded funds)** and **mutual funds** that invest in AI — covered in **Chapter 6**.

### Creating Your Own Prompts: A Simple Formula
The author gives a **two-step formula** for creating a useful prompt (until you get proficient):
1. **Give the AI tool a scenario** as the first part of your prompt.
   - Example: "I am a 60-year-old single woman with $X amount in my 401(k) plan..."
2. **Then give a specific question** as the second part of your prompt.
   - Example: "...and I am considering a growth fund, but I want to compare the pros and cons with a growth-and-income fund."
- Once proficient, explore ChatGPT's expanding uses and add-ons at https://platform.openai.com/examples.

### The Limits and Negatives of ChatGPT
ChatGPT is far from perfect. Key drawbacks/glitches to keep in mind:
- **A limit on knowledge:** ChatGPT 3.5 (free version) has a knowledge cutoff (January 2022 at the time of writing) — it doesn't have real-time data.
- **Bias:** Questions about philosophy, politics, public policy, and related areas where human bias is strong can produce skewed answers. The author notes the "garbage in equals garbage out" saying applies, since humans are involved in both programming AI and supplying its training data.
- **Incorrect answers:** Bugs and glitches mean there's a possibility of errors and poor/inaccurate responses — multiple sources should be checked, especially for sensitive or important topics.
- **Cannot provide personal advice:** AI tools generally don't have access to personal data, so avoid asking personal questions; you can give it scenarios/example problems, but it won't take a mindful, sentient-guidance role like a human expert.
- **Other issues with AI:** The author won't try to be comprehensive about all of AI's failings and shortcomings here — it isn't an emotional being and won't take personal criticism, but he encourages awareness of its limitations.

## Chapter 3: Spanning the AI Landscape: Different Inputs and Outputs

### Overview

### First Things First: Text-to-Text with Chatbots
- ChatGPT (covered in depth in Chapter 2) is an AI designed to interact with users via questions and requests (prompts), returning answers in text format — the most common and familiar AI interaction.
- ChatGPT is described as fluent in languages — for example, you can paste a 500-word Spanish paragraph and have it translated into English (or another language) in seconds.
- The chatbot "role-plays": you can ask for an answer "as if you were a fifth grader," or "as if it were told by Darth Vader, Tarzan, or Donald Duck," demonstrating its flexible, patient, all-knowing-robot character.
- Examples of text-to-text utility:
  - **Language power:** Translate text fluently between languages instantly.
  - **Learn a new skill:** In a competitive economy, the more skills you have, the more employable/valuable you become; AI can teach skills inexpensively from home rather than paying for expensive courses or tutors.
  - **Do programming for you:** Design a webpage in HTML or Python, create Excel formulas your boss needs, all without needing deep technical knowledge — ask a detailed question and get a step-by-step answer in seconds.
  - **What do you imagine you need?** Examples given: a poem for a 30th wedding anniversary (the author's own example — his wife was impressed), a resume in seconds, a business plan, a marketing checklist. "The uses are endless."

### Other AI Inputs and Outputs
Beyond text-to-text, several other AI formats can benefit professional, career, and investing pursuits:

**Document-to-text**
- One of the most useful AI functions in today's information-overloaded age — extracting usable, succinct meaning from voluminous documentation.
- Example tool: **ChatPDF** (www.chatpdf.com) — analyzes or summarizes a lengthy PDF.
- Useful for investors/speculators: reviewing and summarizing online documents such as research reports, investment posts, and other detailed documents.

**Audio-to-text**
- The author does videos on economics, investing, business, and related topics, and uses AI tools to transcribe spoken words into text (for training materials or e-books for clients/students).
- Process: record a lecture (via digital voice recorder or smartphone) as a WAV or MP3 file, then upload it to a transcription service such as **Otter** (www.otter.ai) to get a completed transcript in minutes.
- For investors, audio-to-text AI can turn investing classes and lectures into transcripts for easier review/reference.

**Text-to-image**
- Previously required hiring a graphic designer and waiting hours; now you provide a text description of the desired image and the AI generates it for free or low cost.
- Top text-to-image AI tools listed:
  - **Midjourney** (www.midjourney.com)
  - **DALL-E 3** (https://openai.com/dall-e-3)
  - **Leonardo.Ai** (www.leonardo.ai)
- Use cases:
  - **Business uses:** Creating images for a website or any business need — describe what you want (the text input) and AI produces many image variations. Example given: describing an "ice cream cone" image (chocolate and strawberry, rainbow sprinkles in a waffle cone) and having it generated in seconds.
  - **Financial uses:** Describe how well a given stock, asset, or investment vehicle has performed against a benchmark (e.g., the Dow or S&P 500) to the AI tool, and it can create a chart so you can judge performance visually.

**Text-to-video**
- For personal or business needs, AI can create video from text descriptions (such as an article or post).
- Top tools for this task listed:
  - **Pictory.ai** (www.pictory.ai)
  - **Hour One** (www.hourone.ai)
  - **Flexclip** (www.flexclip.com)

**Image-to-insight / data visualization note**
- A simple picture can be more powerful and useful than a mountain of dry data.
- Example: **Visual Capitalist** (www.visualcapitalist.com) — excellent at sifting, analyzing, and presenting data, taking it one level beyond a simple visual, useful for investment decision-making.
- Anecdote: an analyst noticed that movement in junk bonds was an early precursor to subsequent movement in stocks (the S&P 500) — when junk bonds rose, the S&P 500 followed; when junk bonds declined, the S&P 500 declined too. He found this in a chart (a visual), which was the culmination of extensive data analysis.
- **Tip:** Using AI tools mentioned in the chapter (and Appendix B), readers can have AI extract data for two or more interrelated markets/vehicles to see whether and how closely they correlate (e.g., how closely are bond markets and stock markets correlated across economic cycles? How closely is gold related to the inflation rate?). This is presented as a way AI can serve as an alert system for investing and trading strategies.

# Chapter 4: Large-Cap AI Stocks

## Market capitalization basics
- **Market cap** = share price × shares outstanding. Used to categorize company size.
- **Mega-cap**: >$200 billion
- **Large-cap**: $10 billion–$200 billion
- **Mid-cap**: $2 billion–$10 billion
- **Small-cap**: $250 million–$2 billion
- **Micro-cap**: under $250 million (riskiest, least liquid)

## AI Companies: the major players
Named as of December 2023, the largest companies on the planet, all deeply tied to AI:
- **Apple (AAPL)** — largest company; AI woven into iPhone/Mac/cloud compute, though less publicized than peers.
- **Microsoft (MSFT)** — major OpenAI investor and partner; AI across Office, Azure, Bing, GitHub Copilot.
- **Nvidia (NVDA)** — dominant AI/graphics processing chipmaker; huge run-up from AI infrastructure demand.
- **Alphabet/Google (GOOG/GOOGL)** — search, cloud AI, Gemini, DeepMind.
- **Amazon (AMZN)** — AWS cloud AI infrastructure, Alexa, retail AI/logistics.
- **Meta (META)** — AI in ad targeting, content recommendation, Llama open models.

## Pros and cons of AI stocks for investors
**Pros:**
- **Growth** — AI names, especially large-cap, sit in a long-term secular growth trend.
- **Income** — some large-cap AI companies (e.g. Microsoft, Apple) also pay dividends.
- Diversified players reduce single-product risk vs. pure-play AI startups.

**Cons:**
- Valuations can run far ahead of fundamentals (bubble risk, dot-com comparisons raised).
- Concentration risk — a handful of mega-cap AI names dominate index performance; a pullback in a few names can drag the broader market.
- Regulatory/antitrust exposure for Big Tech AI leaders.

## SWOT analysis for AI investing
Framework: **S**trengths, **W**eaknesses, **O**pportunities, **T**hreats — applied per company or industry before buying. Example demonstrated with a ChatGPT-assisted SWOT of Nvidia, covering competitive moat, customer concentration, and cyclical semiconductor risk.

## Using AI to analyze large-cap stocks
- Use chatbots (ChatGPT, Bing Chat) to pull company fundamentals, summarize 10-K/10-Q filings, and draft comparative analysis prompts.
- Example prompt style: "Compare [company X] to [competitor Y]. Analyze growth, profitability, and valuation using recent financial statements."
- AI tools can summarize SEC filings (10-K annual, 10-Q quarterly) and highlight key risk factors and financial trends quickly.

# Chapter 6: Diversifying with ETFs and Mutual Funds

## Why diversify
- A single stock (even a winner) is riskier than a diversified basket — an ETF covering the same sector will outperform a losing stock but underperform an individual winning stock. Diversification trades some upside for downside protection.

## ETFs vs. mutual funds — shares and purchases/sales
- **ETFs**: trade like stocks throughout the day on an exchange at market price; typically lower expense ratios; tax-efficient due to in-kind creation/redemption process.
- **Mutual funds**: priced once per day at NAV (net asset value) after market close; may have investment minimums; actively managed funds carry higher fees.

## Pros and cons of ETFs and mutual funds

**ETFs — pros:** diversification, flexibility (intraday trading), transparency (holdings disclosed daily), generally lower fees, tax efficiency.
**ETFs — cons:** trading costs (bid-ask spread), potential for overtrading, may not perfectly track sector due to weighting quirks.

**Mutual funds — pros:** professional active management, automatic investment options (good for retirement accounts/401(k)s), convenience.
**Mutual funds — cons:** higher fees/expenses, less tax efficiency, minimum investment requirements, less transparency (holdings disclosed less frequently), no intraday trading, potential lack of investor control over specific holdings.

# Chapter 8: Fundamental and Technical Analysis

## Framing: fundamental vs. technical
- **Long-term investors** should focus primarily on **fundamental analysis**.
- **Short-term traders/speculators** rely more on **technical analysis**.
- Fundamental analysis answers "what" (is this a good company); technical analysis answers "when" (is this a good time to trade it).

## Fundamental analysis
Looks at a company's financial statements, competitive position, industry, and management to judge intrinsic value.

**Key fundamental factors:**
- **Profitability** — is the company generating solid profit margins?
- **Solvency** — can the company meet long-term obligations (assets vs. liabilities)?
- **Multi-year consistency** — look for a stable multi-year track record, not just one good year.
- **The company's market and competition** — assess competitive moat and industry position.

## Fundamental analysis ratios and AI
Key ratios covered, each explainable/calculable via AI chatbot prompts:
1. **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** — net income allocated per share; higher/growing EPS is favorable.
2. **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio** — share price divided by EPS; indicates how much investors pay per dollar of earnings.
3. **Free Cash Flow (FCF)** — cash generated after capital expenditures; shows real cash-generating ability.
4. **Debt-to-Equity Ratio** — measures leverage; higher ratio = more reliance on debt financing.
5. **Return on Equity (ROE)** — net income relative to shareholder equity; measures how efficiently equity is used to generate profit.
6. **Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio** — compares market value to book value; useful for asset-heavy industries.
- Suggested ChatGPT prompt style: "Please explain [ratio] and how it applies to [Company X]'s financials."

## Technical analysis
Chart-based analysis of price and volume patterns for short/medium-term trading decisions; more relevant to traders than long-term investors, though useful supplementary context.

**Key indicators covered:**
- **Moving Averages (SMA/EMA)** — smooths price data to identify trend direction; a spotlight is given to the **Relative Strength Index (RSI)**, a momentum oscillator used to identify overbought/oversold conditions.
- **Bollinger Bands** — volatility bands around a moving average; price touching the bands can signal overbought/oversold conditions.
- **MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)** — trend-following momentum indicator comparing two EMAs.
- **Volume** — confirms strength of price moves; rising price on rising volume is a stronger signal.
- **Average True Range (ATR)** — measures volatility magnitude, useful for setting stop-loss distances.
- **Ichimoku Cloud** — comprehensive Japanese charting indicator showing support/resistance, trend, and momentum in one view.

Technical analysis works best combined with fundamental analysis rather than used alone — book recommends AI tools to help interpret chart patterns and technical indicator readings via natural-language prompts.

# Chapter 9: Hard Assets and Commodities

## Why hard assets and commodities matter
A truly diversified portfolio needs some exposure to assets not dependent on counterparty risk — things tied to actual human "needs" rather than "wants." Commodities and hard assets (gold, silver, other precious metals, agricultural and energy commodities) are this book's chosen complement to stocks/bonds/paper-digital assets.

## Investing versus speculating
- **Investing**: buying an asset for long-term value appreciation/income based on fundamentals.
- **Speculating**: betting on short-term price movement, often with leverage — riskier, less tied to underlying fundamentals.
- Financial media often blur the two; the book urges investors to be clear-eyed about which one they're doing, especially with commodities (which tend to attract speculators).

## Comparing the risks of precious metals and commodities
- **Counterparty risk**: the risk that the other party in a contract (e.g. bond issuer, ETF sponsor) fails to deliver. Precious metals held physically avoid this; commodity futures/ETFs carry some.
- Precious metals and commodities are more volatile and don't generate income (no dividends/interest) — return depends purely on price appreciation.

## Risks with precious metals
- **Price volatility**, storage/security costs for physical holdings, no yield, and market sentiment swings (inflation fears, currency weakness) drive pricing more than fundamentals.
- **Bond default risk** and **currency risk** discussed as reasons investors turn to metals as a hedge against fiat currency devaluation and government/corporate debt risk.
- **Inflation risk** — precious metals often (not always) act as an inflation hedge.

## Handling hard assets (primarily precious metals)
**Ways to invest in gold and silver:**
- Physical bullion (coins, bars) — full ownership, but storage/insurance costs and liquidity friction.
- **ETFs and mutual funds** — convenient, liquid, tracks metal price without physical storage hassle.
- **Futures** — leveraged, higher risk, for more sophisticated/speculative investors.
- **Mining stocks** — indirect exposure; adds company-specific/operational risk on top of metal price risk.

## AI strategies and precious metals
Use AI (ChatGPT) to:
- Get historical performance analysis of metals vs. other asset classes.
- Draft prompts comparing precious metals ETFs on fees/liquidity/tracking accuracy.
- Analyze market sentiment and macro factors (inflation data, rate policy) driving metals prices.

## Considering commodities — resources
Book resources for commodities research: World Gold Council, Silver Institute, precious metals investing sites, and Bloomberg/Kitco-style commodity trackers. Also references *Commodities for Dummies* by Amine Bouchentouf for deeper background.

## Comparing commodities and futures
- **Commodity**: the underlying physical good (raw agricultural, energy, or metal product).
- **Futures contract**: standardized agreement to buy/sell a commodity at a set price on a future date — the primary vehicle for trading/investing in commodities without holding the physical goods.

## Types of commodities (ChatGPT-assisted breakdown)
- **Agricultural commodities**: corn, wheat, soybeans, cotton, sugar, coffee, cocoa.
- **Oilseeds**: soybeans, sunflower seeds.
- **Livestock and meat**: cattle, hogs, feeder cattle.
- **Dairy products**: milk, butter, cheese.
- **Tropical products**: coffee, cocoa, sugar (grown mainly in tropical regions).
- **Industrial (base) metals**: copper, aluminum, zinc, nickel — used in construction/manufacturing.

- **Rare earth metals**: used in electronics/tech manufacturing (specialized, less commonly traded directly by retail investors).
- **Exotic metals**: rhodium, iridium, ruthenium — precious/rare, less commonly traded.

## Looking at different types of commodity investing vehicles
- **Futures and options** — direct commodity exposure, requires more sophistication and risk tolerance.
- **ETFs and mutual funds** — most accessible route for retail investors; convenient diversified commodity exposure without futures account complexity.
- **Agricultural commodities specifically** — every day people encounter these through crops; environmentally conscious investors should weigh both the investment case and broader environmental/social impact of agricultural commodity production.

# Chapter 10: Bonds, Bank Investments, and Digital Currencies

## Why diversify beyond stocks
Even a fully-stock portfolio should shift allocation as circumstances change — e.g. approaching retirement means moving from growth (tech stocks) toward income-producing, lower-risk assets like dividend stocks, bonds, and bank CDs.

## Bonds and bank investments
- A **bond** is a debt instrument — the investor loans money to the issuer (government, municipality, or corporation) in exchange for periodic interest and repayment of principal at maturity.
- Bonds provide diversification and act as ballast against stock market volatility. Bank deposit accounts (savings, CDs, money markets) are the safest category, FDIC-insured up to limits.

## Bond issuers and types
- **Federal government** (Treasury bonds/notes/bills) — safest category.
- **Municipal bonds** — issued by state/local governments, often tax-advantaged.
- **Corporate bonds** — issued by companies, higher yield but higher risk depending on issuer credit quality.

## Bond ratings
- Ratings (AAA down to junk/high-yield) indicate credit quality — higher rating = lower risk = lower yield.
- Sample ChatGPT prompt: "Give me one bond rating in Figure 10-1, then explain it."

## Questions about bonds to ask AI
Suggested prompts to understand bond concepts: explaining specific rating categories, comparing Treasury vs. municipal vs. corporate bonds, and clarifying terms like "yield to maturity" and "duration."

## Bank investments
Types of bank accounts covering the full range of savings vehicles:
- **Savings accounts** — safe, liquid, lower yield.
- **Money market accounts (MMAs)** — higher yield than savings, some liquidity/check-writing.
- **Certificates of Deposit (CDs)** — locked-in rate for a fixed term, penalty for early withdrawal.
- **Checking accounts** — for transactions, minimal/no yield.

## Bond resources
Bonds Online, Treasury Direct (treasurydirect.gov), Best Bond Market Blogs and Websites, FINRA bond center — for research and buying bonds directly.

## Cryptocurrencies
- Cryptocurrencies are a technology and peer-to-peer exchange medium built on blockchain, distinct from traditional bank/government-issued money — carrying added risk and volatility.
- ChatGPT response to "What is cryptocurrency?" explains it as a decentralized digital currency using cryptographic security, not backed by a government or central bank.
- Book urges caution: only invest what you can afford to lose; treat crypto as a small, high-risk allocation, not a core holding — and note cryptocurrencies are not FDIC-insured.

## Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)
- **CBDC**: a digital form of a country's official currency, issued and backed directly by the central bank (unlike decentralized cryptocurrencies).
- Potential benefits: financial inclusion, faster/cheaper payments, reduced settlement risk, enhanced monetary policy transmission.
- Potential negatives/concerns: privacy/surveillance risk (centralized transaction visibility), cybersecurity risk, disintermediation of commercial banks, technology/currency exchange risks during rollout, and regulatory uncertainty.
- CBDCs remain in development/pilot stages in many countries as of the book's writing; the U.S. Federal Reserve has explored the concept but not launched one.

# Chapter 13: Using AI for Economic Analysis

## Why economics matters to investors
The broader economy drives investments, business, career, and retirement outcomes. Understanding key indicators — with AI's help — lets investors act ahead of downturns instead of getting caught off guard (e.g. the 2008 financial crisis).

## Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
- **GDP**: total value of goods/services produced by a nation in a period (typically quarterly/annually). Rising GDP is a positive signal for the economy and stock market; flat/shrinking GDP is a negative signal.
- Use AI (ChatGPT) prompts to pull recent GDP figures, define related terms, and interpret what a given reading means for markets.
- **Key points to breakdown**: GDP components (consumption, investment, government spending, net exports), how GDP growth correlates with corporate earnings and stock performance, and leading vs. lagging nature of the indicator.

## The labor market
- Employment/unemployment data is a key gauge of economic health — strong labor market generally supports consumer spending and corporate revenue.
- Investment strategies differ for a strong vs. weak labor market: strong market favors growth/cyclical stocks; weak market favors defensive stocks, quality bonds, and dividend payers.

## Income taxes
Tax policy changes (personal and corporate) affect take-home pay, corporate profits, and market sentiment — worth tracking as part of macro analysis, with AI able to summarize current tax law changes and their likely market impact.

## Interest rates
- Interest rates affect both borrowers (cost of capital) and the broader economy/financial markets — rate hikes generally cool growth and can pressure stock valuations; rate cuts stimulate borrowing/spending.
- High rates historically correlate with tighter financial conditions; low rates with easier money and asset price inflation.
- AI can pull historical rate data, summarize Fed rate decisions, and help model how a rate change might ripple through specific sectors/investments (see Appendix C for resources).
- Suggested strategies for a rising-rate environment: shorter-duration bonds, dividend-paying quality stocks, defensive sector tilt; for a falling-rate environment: longer-duration bonds, growth stocks.

## Leading Economic Indicators (LEI)
- **LEI**: a composite index (stock market performance, jobless claims, manufacturing orders, and more) designed to predict future economic direction before it fully shows up in GDP data.
- AI can summarize the current LEI reading and its components' individual trends.

## Inflation
- Inflation erodes purchasing power over time; the book distinguishes overall inflation from investment-specific effects — some assets (real assets, TIPS) are natural inflation hedges, others (long-duration bonds) suffer more.
- Suggested prompt style: "What investment strategies are used to hedge against inflation?"

## The Federal Reserve ("the Fed")
- The Fed manages U.S. monetary policy via interest rates, money supply, and bank reserve requirements — a critical driver of both the economy and financial markets.
- **Role of the Fed** covered: managing the money supply, setting the federal funds rate, banking system oversight, and acting as lender of last resort in crises.
- AI can serve as a resource for understanding current Fed policy, summarizing FOMC statements, and explaining monetary policy jargon in plain language.

## The political landscape
- Fiscal policy (government spending, taxation) set by elected officials also materially affects the economy and markets, regardless of political party.
- Investors should evaluate policy proposals' economic impact objectively rather than through a partisan lens; AI can help summarize candidate policy positions and their likely aggregate economic effect.

# Chapter 15: Retirement and Estate Planning

## Digging into retirement planning
Core retirement math regardless of starting age: spend less than you earn, invest/save the difference consistently, and figure out the income needed at your target retirement age to determine how much you need to accrue. Always consult a professional alongside AI tools.

## Your starting point: a personal balance sheet
Building a personal balance sheet is essential before projecting retirement needs:
**Assets**: cash and equivalents, investment accounts (brokerage, retirement), real estate, business ownership stake, personal property of value (vehicles, collectibles).
**Liabilities**: mortgage, other real estate debt, credit card debt, student loans, personal loans, taxes payable.
**Net worth** = assets − liabilities; this snapshot anchors realistic retirement projections.

## The "S-10 approach" and a reality check
A framework for stress-testing retirement income assumptions — projecting whether current savings + planned contributions will realistically hit the target retirement number, using conservative growth-rate assumptions rather than optimistic ones. Example walked through with ChatGPT estimating required monthly investment to hit a target nest egg (e.g. $500,000) over a set number of years at a given rate of return.

## A helpful checklist for retirement (numbered items covered)
Includes: diversify your investment portfolio, spend within a sustainable withdrawal rate, establish an emergency fund even in retirement, review current insurance coverage, review Social Security claiming strategy, understand Medicare enrollment timing, consider healthcare cost planning (including long-term care), calculate current net worth, assess retirement expenses realistically, determine retirement income sources, evaluate part-time work options in retirement, create a withdrawal strategy, plan for required minimum distributions (RMDs), update legal documents, consider tax-efficient withdrawal order across account types, and revisit/adjust the plan regularly.

## Even more AI retirement planning prompts
Sample prompts for going deeper on any checklist item with AI, e.g. asking ChatGPT to explain RMD rules or model a specific withdrawal-rate scenario — encourages using AI as an on-demand tutor for retirement planning concepts (Appendix C has more resources).

## Thinking about estate planning
Estate planning is often more complex/daunting than retirement planning and is essential regardless of net worth — even a modest estate benefits from basic planning to avoid probate complications and ensure wishes are honored.

## Defining "estate planning" and beginning the process
Estate planning = the comprehensive process of organizing your financial and personal affairs to be distributed/managed according to your wishes after death or incapacity. Steps outlined:
1. **Create or Update Your Will** — legal document directing asset distribution and naming guardians for minor children.
2. **Establish a Trust** (if applicable) — for more control over asset distribution, potentially avoiding probate.
3. **Designate Beneficiaries** — ensure retirement accounts, life insurance, etc. have correct beneficiaries listed (these pass outside the will).
4. **Durable Power of Attorney (POA)** — appoints someone to manage financial affairs if you become unable to.
5. **Healthcare Power of Attorney / Healthcare Proxy** — appoints someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if incapacitated.
6. **Create a Living Will/Advance Healthcare Directive** — outlines your medical treatment preferences.
7. **Draft a Living Will or Update Healthcare Preferences** (referenced again for emphasis).
8. **Organize Important Documents** — centralize wills, deeds, titles, insurance policies, account statements.
9. **Review and Update Beneficiaries periodically** — life events (marriage, divorce, births) should trigger a review.
10. **Consider Charitable Contributions** — incorporate charitable giving into the estate plan if desired.
11. **Minimize Estate Taxes** — use available exemptions/strategies to reduce estate tax exposure for larger estates.
12. **Create a Digital Estate Plan** — document digital accounts, passwords, access instructions for an executor.
13. **Plan for Business Succession** — if a business owner, document a succession/ownership transfer plan.
14. **Consider Long-Term Care Planning** — align with retirement chapter's LTC coverage discussion.
15. **Communicate Your Plan** — inform family/executor of the plan's existence and key details to avoid confusion.
16. **Periodically Review and Update the Plan** — life changes should trigger a plan review.
17. **Consider Life Insurance** — evaluate whether life insurance can help provide for loved ones or cover outstanding debts/funeral expenses.
18. **Plan for Funeral and Burial Arrangements** — document preferences in advance.
19. **Secure Your Digital Assets** — create a list of digital accounts, passwords, access instructions.
20. **Preserve Family Heirlooms and Sentimental Items** — specify distribution wishes for sentimental items.

Estate planning is complex and benefits from professional guidance (attorneys, financial advisors) — AI can help organize and explain each step, but a specialized estate-planning AI tool and professional advisor are recommended for execution (see Appendix B/C for resources).

# Chapter 16: Tax Considerations

## Chapter overview
Covers general tax rules every investor should know, plus AI tools/resources for tax help. References IRS.gov as the authoritative source; also lists IRS phone lines (800-TAX-1040 for questions, 800-TAX-FORM for publications) and taxchanges.us for tracking major tax legislation changes.

## Reviewing the tax treatment of different investments
- **Ordinary income**: wages, interest, short-term gains — taxed at your regular marginal tax rate.
- **Capital gains**: profit from selling an investment; taxed differently based on holding period.
- **Interest**: most interest income (bank accounts, most bonds) taxed as ordinary income; municipal bond interest is often federally tax-exempt.
- **Dividends**: "qualified" dividends (most U.S. stock dividends held long enough) get preferential capital-gains-rate treatment; "ordinary" (nonqualified) dividends taxed at regular income rates.

## Determining your best strategy
- **Capital gains** — profit from selling an asset above its purchase price. Split into two categories:
  - **Short-term capital gains**: asset held ≤1 year, taxed at ordinary income rates (higher).
  - **Long-term capital gains**: asset held >1 year, taxed at preferential lower rates (0%/15%/20% depending on income).
- The book emphasizes holding investments over a year when feasible specifically to access lower long-term capital gains rates — a core, deliberate tax-efficiency strategy.

## Minimizing capital gains taxes
- Favor long-term holding periods over short-term trading where possible.
- Use tax-advantaged accounts (401(k), IRA) for frequently-traded strategies to shield gains from immediate taxation.
- Consider timing of sales around your marginal tax bracket for a given year.

## Cutting taxes with capital losses
- **Tax-loss harvesting**: selling losing investments to realize a capital loss that offsets capital gains (and up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year), reducing overall tax bill.
- Watch the **wash-sale rule**: repurchasing a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale disallows the loss for tax purposes.

## Evaluating gain and loss scenarios
Walks through worked numeric examples showing how mixing short-term and long-term gains/losses nets out, and the tax savings possible from proactively harvesting losses before year-end.

## Sharing with the IRS
Basic overview of tax forms and reporting: **Schedule D** (capital gains/losses), **Form 8949** (details of sales), **Schedule B** (interest/dividend income), and **1099 forms** (received from brokerages reporting your investment activity to you and the IRS) — brokerage-issued 1099-B, 1099-DIV, 1099-INT statements are the primary source documents for tax filing.

## Looking at tax deductions for investors
- Table of standard deduction amounts by filing status (single, married filing jointly, etc.) for the relevant tax year.
- **Investment interest expense**: margin loan interest may be deductible against investment income (Schedule A, itemized).
- **Foreign taxes on investments**: foreign tax paid on international holdings may be creditable against U.S. tax liability.
- **The donation of securities**: donating appreciated securities directly to charity avoids capital gains tax on the appreciation while still getting a full fair-market-value deduction.
- **Miscellaneous expenses (Schedule A)**: investment-related expenses may be deductible depending on current tax law (subject to change).
- **Nondeductible items**: personal investment losses on personal-use property, certain fees, and other explicitly excluded items are not deductible.

## Getting tax benefits with retirement accounts
### IRAs
- **Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)**: tax-advantaged retirement savings with annual contribution limits (indexed for inflation).
- **Traditional IRA**: contributions may be tax-deductible now; withdrawals in retirement taxed as ordinary income.
- **Roth IRA**: contributions made with after-tax dollars; qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. Income limits apply to Roth eligibility; contribution limits are the same across Traditional/Roth combined.

### 401(k) plans
- Employer-sponsored retirement plans with higher contribution limits than IRAs; many employers offer matching contributions (essentially free money).
- Traditional 401(k) contributions are pre-tax (reduce current taxable income); Roth 401(k) contributions are after-tax with tax-free qualified withdrawals.

## Checking out AI tax tactics and strategies
The bottom line: AI is a useful tool for surfacing general tax-saving strategies and summarizing tax rules, but it's not a substitute for a qualified tax professional (CPA, enrolled agent) for actual filing and complex situations.

## Tapping into tax resources
- **IRS publications**: free downloadable guides (Publication 550 for investment income, and others) covering specific topics in depth.
- **Tax sites**: TaxFoundation.org, Tax Policy Center, Nolo.com — general tax law reference and explainer sites.
- **AI tax tools**: several named AI-powered tax-prep assistants that can answer questions and help identify deductions.
- **Tax software**: mainstream filing software (TurboTax, H&R Block, etc.) that increasingly integrates AI-assisted guidance during the filing process.

# Chapter 17: Ten Cautions about Artificial Intelligence

## Inaccuracy and errors
AI is not infallible — a perfect real-world example of "garbage in, garbage out." AI can give wrong answers even to simple factual questions (e.g. miscalculating a square root), and has produced inaccurate answers on history, politics, and economics topics the author already knew well. Always verify important AI output.

## Bias
AI models can reflect the biases present in their training data. Example: asking AI to describe a "communist country" produced a slanted response influenced by the AI's training data leanings. Cross-check politically or socially sensitive answers against multiple sources.

## Fraud
Bad actors can use AI to generate convincing scams, fake content, or impersonation material — stay alert to AI-enabled fraud schemes when evaluating investment pitches or unsolicited outreach (see Appendix B for fraud-prevention resources).

## Job displacement
AI adoption may displace some jobs while creating others; the book urges readers to view AI as a tool to make themselves more valuable/indispensable rather than only as a threat (ties back to the career-focused Chapter 12 discussion).

## Overreliance on technology
Don't blindly trust AI as the final word — critically evaluate its output, especially for consequential financial decisions. AI should complement, not replace, human judgment and professional advice.

## Use of the right AI tool
Different AI tools have different specialties — general chatbots (ChatGPT) vs. specialized financial/investing AI tools. Match the tool to the specific task for the best results.

## Your input versus AI output
The quality of AI's response depends heavily on the quality/specificity of your prompt. Vague inputs produce vague or generic outputs — be as specific as possible.

## Faulty predictions
AI-generated forecasts (market predictions, economic forecasts) are not guaranteed to be accurate — treat AI predictions as one input among many, not certainty.

## Privacy
Being careful with what personal/financial information you share with AI tools — assume interactions could be tracked, monitored, or discoverable. Be mindful of data privacy regulations and practice healthy self-monitoring of what you share with AI (including in professional/financial contexts).

# Chapter 18: Ten or So Types of Prompts You Can Use for AI Investing

Overview: these are starter prompts for AI investing use, not a comprehensive list — meant to spark creative, specific prompting.

- **Simple Questions** — direct, single-topic prompts (e.g. "What is a common stock?", "What is a closed-end fund?").
- **Complex Questions** — multi-part or comparative prompts (e.g. comparing a covered call option strategy vs. a covered put option).
- **Formulas** — asking AI to calculate/explain financial formulas using your own numeric inputs (e.g. dividend payout ratio for a described scenario).
- **Lists** — asking AI to generate a checklist or list of considerations for a decision (e.g. a due-diligence checklist before buying a stock).
- **Personal Scenarios with Goals in Mind** — giving AI a concrete personal scenario (income, goal amount, timeframe) and asking for a tailored calculation or plan.
- **Second Opinions** — using AI as a sounding board to critique or validate an investment strategy/approach before committing.
- **Pros and Cons** — asking AI to lay out pros/cons in table format for a specific investment vehicle or decision.
- **Comparisons of Performance** — asking AI to compare the performance of one asset/vehicle against a benchmark (e.g. a stock vs. the S&P 500 over a stated period).
- **Prompts in Different Languages** — AI tools can translate and respond in other languages, useful for non-English-language research or documents.
- **Document Analysis** — pasting/attaching financial documents (10-Ks, prospectuses) for AI to summarize or analyze.
- **Prompts at a Basic Level** — even simple, foundational prompts ("Explain what a stock is like I'm new to investing") are valuable starting points; check Appendix A for more tutorial resources.

# Chapter 20: Ten Strategies to Complement Your AI Investing Pursuits

The chapter frames these as diversification/safety strategies to balance AI-driven investing — avoiding overdependence or overexposure to any single vehicle or theme.

1. **Focusing on Fundamentals** — remember that not every AI-related startup will succeed; grounding stock selection in fundamental analysis (Chapter 8) remains essential even amid AI hype.
2. **Investing in Utilities** — a traditionally defensive sector; utilities tend to be more stable and often pay dividends, providing ballast against volatile growth/AI stock exposure.
3. **Investing in Consumer Staples** — another defensive sector (necessities people buy regardless of economic conditions); historically steadier than growth stocks during downturns.
4. **Considering Healthcare** — a sector with steady long-term demand tied to demographics, offering diversification benefit alongside AI-heavy growth allocations.
5. **Investing in "Safe Harbor" Sectors** — a broader category encompassing utilities, consumer staples, and healthcare together as lower-volatility complements to AI stock exposure.
6. **Choosing the Right Savings Bonds** — Treasury/government-backed savings bonds as a low-risk diversification component, especially for capital preservation goals.
7. **Diversifying with Hard Assets** — allocating to precious metals and other hard assets (Chapter 9) as a counterbalance to paper/digital and AI-related holdings.
8. **Limiting Your Losses** — using stop-loss orders and disciplined position sizing to cap downside on any single volatile AI-related holding.
9. **Personal Preparedness** — keep cash on hand and have a plan (backup power, paper records) for disruptions like power outages, given increasing societal/AI reliance on electricity; check ready.gov for guidance.
10. **Keeping a Paper Trail** — don't rely 100% on digital records; maintain redundant backups (external drives, PDFs in multiple locations, cloud/VPN backup services) in case of data loss, hacking, or access disruptions.
